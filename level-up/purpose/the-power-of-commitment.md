### The Gap Between Interest and Commitment

There is a gap between wanting to do something and deciding to do it. The gap looks small from the outside. From the inside, it is a canyon.

Interest says: I would like to write a book someday. Commitment says: I am writing a book, and it will be finished by December.

Interest says: I care about open source. Commitment says: I contribute three hours every week to this project, and I have for the past year.

Interest says: I want to be a better developer. Commitment says: I study one hour a day, I ship one project per quarter, and I seek feedback after every code review.

The gap is not about effort. Interest can produce tremendous effort — sporadic, intense, fueled by enthusiasm. The gap is about continuity. Interest produces bursts. Commitment produces a line. The line is the difference between a collection of moments and a trajectory.

The reason interest is not enough is simple. Interest is dependent on motivation. Motivation fluctuates. It rises with novelty and falls with difficulty. When the work becomes boring, repetitive, or hard, interest disappears. The project stalls. The book remains unwritten. The contribution remains sporadic. The growth stops.

Commitment survives the fluctuations. It does not depend on motivation. It depends on a decision that was made before the difficulty arrived. The decision is the anchor. The difficulty is the weather. The anchor holds regardless of the weather.

```python
def interest_vs_commitment():
    interest = {
        "trigger": "motivation",
        "response_to_difficulty": "abandon or pause",
        "consistency": "sporadic",
        "identity": "I want to be",
        "time_horizon": "until it gets hard",
    }

    commitment = {
        "trigger": "decision",
        "response_to_difficulty": "persist or adapt",
        "consistency": "daily practice",
        "identity": "I am",
        "time_horizon": "until the commitment is fulfilled",
    }

    # Interest collapses under pressure.
    # Commitment evolves under pressure.
    # The difference is not strength.
    # It is structure.
```

The structure that commitment provides is the most underrated advantage in any pursuit. The structure eliminates the daily negotiation — should I do this today? The negotiation is exhausting and the answer is almost always no when you rely on willpower alone. Commitment removes the negotiation. The question is no longer should I. The question is how.

### What Commitment Actually Is

Commitment is a binding decision made in advance of the full information you wish you had. It is a promise to yourself — and sometimes to others — that you will pursue a specific course of action for a defined period, regardless of fluctuations in motivation, interest, or circumstances.

The key elements of commitment are specificity, duration, and binding force.

Specificity means the commitment defines what you will do. "I will write" is not a commitment. "I will write 500 words every weekday morning before work" is. The specificity eliminates ambiguity. You know whether you fulfilled the commitment. There is no room for self-deception.

Duration means the commitment has a timeframe. "I will do this forever" is not a commitment — it is a fantasy. "I will do this for one year" is. The duration allows you to evaluate the commitment without the terror of permanence. It also makes the commitment bearable. You can endure almost anything for a defined period.

Binding force means the commitment has consequences for breaking it. A commitment without binding force is a wish. The binding force can be internal — the disappointment you feel when you break a promise to yourself — or external — the accountability of a public declaration. The binding force is what separates commitment from intention.

### Why Commitment Is Scary

Commitment is scary because it eliminates options. Before commitment, every path is possible. After commitment, one path is chosen and the others are closed. The closure feels like a loss, even when the chosen path is better than the alternatives.

The fear of closing options is called FOMO — fear of missing out — but the reality is deeper than the acronym suggests. The fear is existential. It is the fear that by choosing one thing, you will miss the life you would have had if you had chosen differently. The life you would have had is imaginary, but the fear is real.

The fear is also about identity. Once you commit, you are the person who does this thing. The identity is constraining. Before commitment, you are undefined — full of potential. After commitment, you are defined — reduced to a choice. The reduction feels like a death of possibility.

But the death of possibility is also a birth of reality. The undefined person has potential but no trajectory. The defined person has a trajectory. The trajectory is where meaning comes from. Meaning is not found in the infinite. It is found in the specific. The commitment is what makes the specific possible.

The fear does not disappear when you commit. It changes. The fear of missing out is replaced by the fear of failing at the thing you chose. The second fear is productive. It keeps you engaged. It drives you to do the work well. The first fear was paralyzing. The second fear is motivating.

### The Elimination of Options

The elimination of options is not a side effect of commitment. It is the mechanism that makes commitment work.

When you have infinite options, you have infinite distractions. Every moment is a choice between the thing you committed to and the ten thousand things you did not. The choice is exhausting, and the non-committed option usually wins because it is novel and the committed option is routine.

When you eliminate options, you eliminate the daily negotiation. The decision was made in the past — when you were clear-eyed, motivated, and rational. The present moment benefits from the past decision. You do not need to decide again. You need to execute.

The elimination is a form of self-care. You are protecting your future self from the weakness of your present self. The present self is vulnerable to distraction, discomfort, and doubt. The future self — the one who will face a Tuesday morning with low energy and a hard task — needs the protection of a prior commitment.

The developer who commits to open source eliminates the option of spending that time on other projects. The developer who commits to learning Rust eliminates the option of spending that time on JavaScript frameworks. The elimination is painful in the moment. It is liberating in the aggregate. The eliminated options were never going to be pursued anyway. The commitment makes the choice honest.

### Kierkegaard's Leap

Søren Kierkegaard, the Danish philosopher, wrote about the "leap of faith" — the idea that the most important decisions in life require a jump into uncertainty. You cannot know the outcome before you commit. You cannot gather enough evidence to guarantee success. At some point, you must leap.

The leap applies directly to commitment. You cannot know whether the direction you chose is the right one. You cannot know whether the effort will pay off. You cannot know whether you will succeed. The uncertainty is permanent. The commitment is the leap.

The leap is not blind. You have gathered data through exploration. You have clarified your values. You have tested the direction through experimentation. The data supports the commitment. But the data does not guarantee the outcome. The guarantee does not exist. The leap is the acceptance of that absence.

Kierkegaard's insight is that the leap itself is meaningful. The act of committing — of choosing one direction despite uncertainty — is an act of self-creation. You become the person who chose. The person who chose is different from the person who waited. The difference is not the outcome. It is the character.

The leap is also the moment when life stops happening to you and starts being shaped by you. Before the leap, you are a passenger. After the leap, you are a driver. The driving may take you somewhere unexpected. But you are driving. That is what matters.

### Commitment Without Guarantees

The mature understanding of commitment is that it is a bet, not a guarantee. You commit knowing that you might fail. You commit knowing that the outcome is uncertain. You commit knowing that circumstances may change in ways that make the original commitment irrelevant.

This understanding is not defeatist. It is realistic. Every commitment in history was made without a guarantee. The scientist who commits to a research program does not know if the hypothesis is correct. The entrepreneur who commits to a business does not know if it will succeed. The artist who commits to a body of work does not know if it will be valued.

The absence of a guarantee is what makes commitment an act of courage rather than an act of calculation. If the outcome were guaranteed, commitment would be mere execution. The courage comes from the uncertainty. The meaning comes from the courage.

The practical implication is that you commit to the process, not the outcome. You commit to writing, not to becoming a bestselling author. You commit to mentoring, not to transforming every mentee. You commit to building, not to the project's success. The process is within your control. The outcome is not. The commitment to the process is the only commitment that makes sense.

### How to Make Commitments You Can Keep

Not all commitments are equal. The commitments that fail are usually too vague, too ambitious, or too unbounded. The commitments that succeed share specific characteristics.

The first characteristic is specificity. The commitment defines exactly what you will do, when you will do it, and for how long. "I will write" is a wish. "I will write 500 words every weekday from 6:30 to 7:30 AM for the next twelve months" is a commitment. The specificity eliminates ambiguity and makes the commitment testable.

The second characteristic is proportionality. The commitment matches your current capacity. You do not commit to running a marathon when you have never run a mile. You commit to running one mile, three times a week. The commitment is achievable. The achievement builds the capacity for a larger commitment.

The third characteristic is flexibility within structure. The commitment defines what you will do but allows variation in how you do it. "I will write 500 words every weekday" does not specify what you will write. The flexibility prevents the commitment from becoming a prison. You maintain autonomy within the commitment.

The fourth characteristic is review points. The commitment has built-in moments for evaluation. "I will write for twelve months, and I will evaluate at three months, six months, and twelve months." The review points allow you to adjust the commitment based on what you have learned. They prevent the commitment from becoming a blind obligation.

The fifth characteristic is accountability. The commitment is witnessed by at least one other person. The witness does not enforce the commitment. They observe it. The observation creates a gentle pressure that supports follow-through.

### The Anatomy of a Real Commitment

A real commitment has structure. The structure is simple but essential.

The commitment statement defines what you will do. It is specific, positive, and actionable. "I will contribute to open-source projects three hours per week" is a commitment statement. "I want to contribute more to open source" is not.

The duration defines how long you will maintain the commitment. One year is the minimum for meaningful commitment. Shorter durations produce data but not depth. Longer durations produce depth but may feel paralyzing. One year is the sweet spot for most commitments.

The accountability structure defines who will witness your commitment. The witness can be a person, a group, or even a public record. The important thing is that the commitment is not private. Private commitments are the easiest to break because no one knows when you break them.

The evaluation criteria define how you will measure success. The criteria are process-based, not outcome-based. "I wrote 500 words per weekday" is a process metric. "I published a bestseller" is an outcome metric. The process metric is within your control. The outcome metric is not. The evaluation must be based on what you can control.

The exit conditions define when and how you will end the commitment. The exit conditions are important because they prevent the commitment from becoming an obligation that persists past its usefulness. "I will end the commitment if I discover that the direction no longer aligns with my values" is an exit condition. The exit conditions honor the fact that circumstances change.

### Commitment Devices

A commitment device is a mechanism that makes it harder to break a commitment than to keep it. The device works by raising the cost of failure.

The simplest commitment device is a calendar. You block time for the commitment. The blocked time creates a visual reminder and a structural anchor. The time is yours. The commitment occupies it.

A more powerful commitment device is a financial commitment. You put money at stake. If you fulfill the commitment, you keep the money. If you break it, the money goes to a cause you oppose. The financial penalty is not about the money. It is about the emotional weight of losing it.

The most powerful commitment device is social commitment. You tell people what you are doing. You publish your progress publicly. You create expectations that you must meet. The social pressure is uncomfortable. It is also effective. The fear of public failure is one of the most reliable motivators available.

```python
def commitment_device(commitment, device_type):
    """Commitment devices increase the cost of breaking a commitment.
    Higher cost = higher probability of follow-through."""

    devices = {
        "calendar": {
            "cost_of_breaking": "disappointment",
            "effectiveness": "low",
            "best_for": "building initial habit",
        },
        "financial": {
            "cost_of_breaking": "monetary loss",
            "effectiveness": "medium",
            "best_for": "short-term motivation",
        },
        "social": {
            "cost_of_breaking": "reputational damage",
            "effectiveness": "high",
            "best_for": "long-term commitment",
        },
        "identity": {
            "cost_of_breaking": "self-betrayal",
            "effectiveness": "highest",
            "best_for": "core values alignment",
        },
    }

    device = devices[device_type]
    return f"Cost of breaking: {device['cost_of_breaking']}. " \
           f"Effectiveness: {device['effectiveness']}. " \
           f"Best for: {device['best_for']}."
```

The most effective commitment device is identity. When a commitment becomes part of who you are, the cost of breaking it is self-betrayal. The identity device is the strongest because it does not depend on external enforcement. It is internal. It travels with you. It cannot be removed.

### Public Declarations and Accountability

The public declaration is one of the oldest commitment devices. You state your commitment in the presence of others. The statement creates a social contract. You have said you will do something. People know. The knowledge creates expectation. The expectation creates pressure. The pressure supports the commitment.

The public declaration works because it engages social identity. You do not want to be the person who says they will do something and then does not. The fear of being perceived as unreliable is a powerful motivator. It is not vanity. It is social accountability.

The accountability relationship is the complement to the public declaration. You find a person who also has commitments. You check in with each other regularly. You report progress honestly. You support each other through difficulty. The accountability relationship is not a friendship. It is a partnership in commitment.

The accountability partner does not enforce your commitment. They witness it. The witnessing is the mechanism. When someone sees your progress, the progress becomes real. When someone sees your struggle, the struggle becomes shared. The sharing reduces the isolation that makes commitment difficult.

The most effective accountability structures have three features: regular check-ins (weekly is optimal), honest reporting (no self-deception), and consequences for missed check-ins (the partner follows up, not to punish, but to re-engage).

### Skin in the Game

Skin in the game is the principle that commitment requires investment. The investment creates stakes. The stakes create motivation.

The investment can be time, money, reputation, or effort. The form does not matter. What matters is that you have something to lose. The loss is the mechanism that makes the commitment real.

Without skin in the game, commitment is theoretical. You can say you care about something without investing in it. The saying costs nothing. The investment costs everything. The investment is the difference between caring and committing.

The developer who puts skin in the game opens a repository and starts committing code. The developer who does not puts skin in the game talks about opening a repository. The difference is the investment. The investment makes the commitment tangible. The tangibility creates momentum.

The skin in the game principle also works in reverse. When you have invested significantly in a commitment, the investment itself becomes a motivator. You have put too much in to quit. The investment creates a form of inertia that supports the commitment. This is the positive face of the sunk cost phenomenon — the investment creating commitment, rather than the investment creating obligation.

### The Relationship Between Commitment and Identity

Commitment and identity are locked in a feedback loop. Commitment shapes identity, and identity sustains commitment.

When you commit to something, you begin to see yourself as the person who does that thing. The identity shift is gradual. You start by doing. The doing becomes a habit. The habit becomes a practice. The practice becomes an identity. "I write" becomes "I am a writer." "I contribute" becomes "I am an open-source contributor." "I teach" becomes "I am a teacher."

The identity shift is powerful because it changes your decision-making. Once you identify as a writer, you make choices that writers make. You read differently. You notice language differently. You carry a notebook. The identity produces behavior that reinforces the identity. The feedback loop is self-sustaining.

The reverse is also true. When you abandon a commitment, the identity erodes. "I used to write" becomes "I am not a writer." The erosion is painful because it is a loss of self. The pain of identity loss is one of the strongest deterrents to breaking commitments.

The relationship between commitment and identity explains why commitment feels like such a big deal. It is not just about the activity. It is about who you become. The commitment is an investment in a future self. The future self is the reward.

### When to Commit and When to Wait

Not every moment is the right moment for commitment. Premature commitment — committing before you have enough data — is as dangerous as the failure to commit. The timing matters.

You are ready to commit when you have enough data from exploration to make an informed decision. Enough data means you have tried the direction, you have seen it under difficulty, and you still want to continue. It does not mean you have no doubts. It means the doubts are manageable and the direction still feels right.

You are ready to commit when your values support the commitment. The alignment between values and commitment is the foundation. A commitment that violates your values will eventually collapse under the weight of the violation. The values must come first.

You are ready to commit when the cost of not committing is higher than the cost of committing. The cost of not committing is continued drift, continued uncertainty, continued exploration without direction. The cost of committing is narrowed focus, eliminated options, and the risk of being wrong. When the drift is more painful than the risk, you are ready.

You should wait when you have not explored enough. The impulse to commit before exploration is complete is driven by the discomfort of uncertainty, not by genuine readiness. The discomfort is real, but the premature commitment is worse.

You should wait when your values are unclear. If you do not know what you care about, you cannot commit to something that serves what you care about. Values clarification must precede commitment.

You should wait when the stakes are high and the data is thin. Some commitments — career changes, relocation, major financial investments — have consequences that cannot be easily reversed. These commitments deserve more data, more reflection, and more time.

### The Sunk Cost Fallacy vs. Genuine Commitment

The sunk cost fallacy is the tendency to continue investing in something because of past investment, even when the investment is no longer justified. The fallacy is one of the most common errors in commitment.

The fallacy sounds like this: I have been working on this for three years. I cannot quit now. The three years are the sunk cost. They are gone regardless of what you do next. The decision should be based on the future, not the past. If the future does not justify the investment, the past does not save it.

Genuine commitment is different from sunk cost persistence. Genuine commitment is a decision to continue based on the value of the direction, not the cost of the past. The genuine commitment is forward-looking. The sunk cost fallacy is backward-looking.

The distinction is not always clear. Sometimes the reason to continue is both the value of the direction and the investment already made. The investment is not always irrelevant — it may represent skills, relationships, or knowledge that make continuation more productive than starting fresh.

The test is to ask: if I were starting today, knowing what I know now, would I choose this direction? If the answer is yes, the commitment is genuine. If the answer is no, the persistence is sunk cost. The answer requires honesty. The honesty is the hardest part.

### Re-Committing: Refreshing Your Commitment Over Time

Commitments are not permanent. They require periodic renewal. The renewal is not a sign of weakness. It is a sign of integrity.

The renewal process asks: does this commitment still serve my values? Has my understanding of the direction changed? Is the commitment still the right level of ambition? The questions are honest. The answers may require adjustment.

The adjustment can take several forms. You might renew the commitment as-is. The direction still fits. The commitment is working. The renewal is a confirmation.

You might revise the commitment. The direction still fits but the specifics need adjustment. You increase or decrease the scope. You change the timeframe. You modify the accountability structure. The revision is an evolution.

You might end the commitment. The direction no longer fits. Your values have changed. The commitment has served its purpose. The ending is not a failure. It is a graduation.

The practice of re-committing prevents the commitment from becoming an obligation that persists past its usefulness. The obligation is the shadow side of commitment. It turns the commitment from a choice into a burden. The renewal restores the choice.

The optimal renewal cycle is annual. Once a year, you revisit every significant commitment. You ask the hard questions. You make the necessary adjustments. The annual renewal keeps your commitments aligned with your growth.

### Commitment in Relationships, Career, and Creative Work

Commitment operates in every domain of life, and the principles are the same across all of them.

In relationships, commitment means choosing a person and building with them through difficulty. The commitment is not the feeling of love. The feeling fluctuates. The commitment is the decision to stay, to work, to grow together despite the fluctuations. The decision is renewed daily, even when it does not feel like a decision.

In career, commitment means choosing a direction and building expertise over years. The commitment is not to a specific job or company. It is to a trajectory. The trajectory may include multiple jobs and companies, but the direction is consistent. The consistency produces depth that job-hopping cannot.

In creative work, commitment means showing up for the practice regardless of inspiration. The commitment is to the process, not the product. You write when you do not feel like writing. You code when you do not feel like coding. You create when the muse is absent. The showing up is the commitment. The product is the byproduct.

The common thread across all domains is that commitment transforms the temporary into the enduring. Without commitment, relationships are serial. Careers are scattered. Creative work is sporadic. With commitment, relationships deepen, careers compound, and creative work accumulates into a body of work.

### The Developer's Commitments

Developers make specific commitments that shape their careers and their craft.

The commitment to craft is the commitment to writing code that is not just functional but well-made. The commitment means learning best practices, studying patterns, and caring about readability. It means spending extra time on the code review, the test, the documentation. It means treating the codebase as a garden, not a construction site.

The commitment to users is the commitment to building things that actually help people. The commitment means listening to feedback, understanding problems, and solving them rather than building what you want to build. It means empathy as a technical skill.

The commitment to growth is the commitment to never being done learning. The commitment means reading, studying, experimenting, and seeking discomfort. It means accepting that the field changes and that staying relevant requires constant evolution. It means that the senior developer who stops learning is really a junior developer with seniority.

The commitment to community is the commitment to contributing beyond your immediate work. The commitment means open-source contributions, mentoring, knowledge sharing, and maintaining the commons. It means giving back more than you take.

These commitments are not separate. They form an integrated practice. The commitment to craft produces better products for users. The commitment to growth produces better craft. The commitment to community produces growth through teaching. The commitments reinforce each other. Together, they form the architecture of a meaningful career.

### A Walkthrough: Making a Life-Changing Commitment

Here is a concrete walkthrough of someone making a commitment. The commitment is to transition from a salaried developer to an independent consultant.

**The data.** Two years of exploration have produced clarity. The developer values autonomy (core), craftsmanship (core), and teaching (supporting). The current job provides security but violates autonomy — the developer has no control over projects, priorities, or schedule. The violation produces resentment that grows annually.

**The fear.** The developer is afraid of losing the salary. The fear is rational — the mortgage is real, the children need food, the benefits matter. The fear is also paralyzing. It has kept the developer in a misaligned job for three years.

**The preparation.** The developer saves six months of expenses. The savings reduce the financial risk from catastrophic to manageable. The developer builds a client list through part-time consulting. The clients provide evidence that the market values their skills. The developer writes a business plan. The plan is simple — three paragraphs, not fifty pages.

**The commitment.** The developer writes a commitment statement: "I will transition to independent consulting within six months. I will give notice at my current job when I have three months of contracted revenue. I will maintain my craft by contributing to open source ten hours per week. I will evaluate the commitment at six months and twelve months."

**The accountability.** The developer shares the commitment with their partner, a trusted friend, and a professional network. The sharing creates social accountability. The partner provides emotional support. The friend provides honest feedback. The network provides referrals.

**The execution.** The developer begins the transition. The first month is terrifying. The second month is productive. The third month is exhilarating. The fourth month is hard — a client falls through, and the financial fear returns. The commitment holds. The developer finds a new client within two weeks. The sixth month arrives. The developer evaluates. The commitment is renewed.

The walkthrough illustrates the structure of commitment: data, fear, preparation, statement, accountability, execution, and evaluation. The structure does not eliminate difficulty. It makes difficulty navigable.

### The Other Side of Commitment

Commitment has a reward that is not discussed enough. On the other side of commitment is freedom.

The freedom is paradoxical. You would expect that eliminating options reduces freedom. It does. But it also produces a different kind of freedom — freedom from the daily negotiation, freedom from the paralysis of infinite choice, freedom from the question "what should I do?"

Once you commit, you know what you are doing. The knowing is a form of liberation. You stop spending energy on deciding and start spending it on doing. The doing is where satisfaction comes from. The doing is where meaning comes from. The doing is where growth comes from.

The freedom is also creative. Within the commitment, there is infinite space. The commitment defines the boundaries. Inside the boundaries, you are free to explore, experiment, and create. The boundaries do not constrain creativity. They focus it. The focused creativity produces deeper work than the unfocused variety.

The freedom is also temporal. The commitment frees you from the tyranny of the present moment. You do not need to decide what to do with every hour. The commitment has already decided. The decision is made. The hours have a structure. The structure is the scaffold that supports the work.

### When Commitment Breaks

Commitments break. The breaking is painful. It should be — the breaking represents the failure to uphold a promise you made to yourself.

The reasons for breaking are varied. Sometimes the commitment was poorly made — too ambitious, too vague, or misaligned with actual values. Sometimes circumstances change in ways that make the commitment irrelevant. Sometimes the commitment was right but the execution failed.

The response to broken commitment matters more than the breaking itself. The response can be shame, which leads to avoidance, which leads to the abandonment of commitment altogether. Or the response can be analysis, which leads to understanding, which leads to better commitment in the future.

The analysis asks: why did the commitment break? Was it the commitment or the execution? Was it the direction or the specifics? Was it internal (loss of motivation, misalignment) or external (life change, circumstance)? The answers guide the revision.

The developer who breaks a commitment to write every day might discover that the daily cadence was too ambitious. The revision is a commitment to write three times per week. The developer who breaks a commitment to learn a new language might discover that the language was not connected to any real project. The revision is a commitment to learn a language that serves an actual goal.

### Starting Again After Broken Commitment

Starting again after a broken commitment is one of the hardest things to do. The broken commitment creates a narrative of failure. The narrative says: you are the kind of person who does not follow through. The narrative is powerful and wrong.

The narrative is wrong because it treats commitment as binary — keep or break. The reality is that commitment is iterative. You commit, you break, you learn, you re-commit. The iteration is the practice. The practice produces growth. The growth produces better commitment.

Starting again requires self-forgiveness. The forgiveness is not about lowering standards. It is about accuracy. The accurate assessment is: you made a commitment, you broke it, and you learned something. The learning is valuable. The value is wasted if it becomes shame instead of insight.

The practice of starting again is simple. You write a new commitment. The new commitment incorporates the lessons of the broken one. You share it with your accountability partner. You begin. The beginning is the only part that matters. Everything before it is regret. Everything after it is progress.

The developer who broke a commitment to contribute to open source starts again with a smaller commitment — one pull request per month. The developer who broke a commitment to study daily starts again with a commitment to study on weekends. The new commitment is not a retreat. It is a recalibration. The recalibration is wisdom.

## Learning Tips

- Start with a commitment so small it feels trivial. "I will write one sentence every day." The small commitment builds the muscle of commitment. The muscle grows. The commitment grows with it.
- Track your commitment fulfillment visually. A calendar with check marks. A spreadsheet with streaks. The visual evidence of consistency is motivating. The streak becomes its own reward.
- Tell someone about your commitment on the day you make it. The immediacy of the declaration prevents the delay that kills commitment. The delay is where commitments go to die.
- Review your commitment monthly, not daily. Daily review produces anxiety. Monthly review produces perspective. The perspective supports the commitment.
- When you break a commitment, restart within twenty-four hours. The longer you wait, the harder the restart. The restart is the commitment to the commitment.
