# Principles of Clear Writing

## Description

Clear writing is a form of clear thinking. Whether you're writing a README, a design doc, a commit message, or a code comment, the same principles apply: know your audience, state your purpose, and cut everything that doesn't serve it. This document covers the timeless principles that underpin all good technical communication.

## Content / Material

### Clarity Over Cleverness

The goal of technical writing is not to impress — it is to transfer understanding. A simple sentence the reader understands once is better than a clever sentence they have to reread.

| Instead of | Write |
|------------|-------|
| "Utilize" | "Use" |
| "In the event that" | "If" |
| "A multiplicity of" | "Many" |
| "Endeavor to ascertain" | "Find out" |

### Structure First

Before writing a single sentence, decide the structure. Good structure is invisible — bad structure makes content impenetrable.

- Start with the **conclusion** or **purpose** (don't bury the lede).
- Group related ideas.
- Use headings, lists, and tables to create visual hierarchy.
- One paragraph = one idea.

### Precision Over Volume

Every extra word is noise. Write, then cut 20%. Then cut 10% more.

```
Before: "The purpose of this document is to provide an overview of the various factors that should be considered when selecting a database for your application."

After:  "This document helps you choose a database for your application."
```

### The Reader's Time

Assume your reader is busy. They want the answer, not the journey.

- Put the most important information first (inverted pyramid).
- Use summaries for long documents.
- Link to details instead of including them inline.
- If it can be a table or a list, make it a table or a list.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Inverted pyramid | A writing structure where the most important information comes first |
| Visual hierarchy | The arrangement of elements to indicate importance through formatting |
| Buried lede | Hiding the main point inside supporting text rather than stating it upfront |
