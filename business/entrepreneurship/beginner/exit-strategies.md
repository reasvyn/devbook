# Exit Strategies

## Description

Exit strategies cover how founders and investors realize returns from a startup — acquisition, IPO, secondary sales, or orderly shutdown. Understanding exit options early shapes strategic decisions about business model, fundraising, and corporate structure.

## Content / Material

### Why Exit Strategy Matters

Exit strategy is not just about how the founder gets paid. It shapes every major decision: what business model you pursue, how much capital you raise, what investors you take, and how you build the company.

**The three exit truths:**

1. **Most startups don't exit for billions.** The vast majority exit for modest amounts ($10M-$200M) or nothing. Plan accordingly.
2. **Exit is not failure.** A successful exit at any size that returns capital to investors and creates value for acquirers is a win.
3. **Exit readiness is company health.** A company that can be acquired is a well-run company: clean books, strong team, clear IP, growing market.

**Exit shapes strategy:**

| Exit Path | Strategy Implications |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Acquisition** | Build something valuable to a specific acquirer. Focus on strategic fit, not just revenue |
| **IPO** | Build for public markets. Focus on compliance, predictable growth, large TAM |
| **Lifestyle / Dividend** | Build a profitable company. Focus on sustainable margins, no VC pressure |
| **Secondary** | Provide liquidity for founders and employees without selling the company |

**When to think about exit:**

From day one, structure the company as if it will be acquired. Clean cap table, proper IP assignments, auditable financials. These things are hard to fix later and acquirers check them all.

### Types of Exits

| Exit Type | Description | Typical Outcome | Timeframe |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Acquisition** | Company is bought by another company | $10M-$1B+ | 3-10 years |
| **IPO** | Shares sold on public market | $100M-$10B+ | 7-15 years |
| **Merger** | Two companies combine | Equity in new entity | 3-10 years |
| **Secondary sale** | Shares sold to new investors | Partial liquidity | 2-7 years |
| **Dividend recap** | Company takes debt to pay shareholders | Cash to shareholders | 5-10 years |
| **Shutdown** | Assets sold, company dissolved | Usually zero for equity | 1-5 years |

**Acquisition is the most likely outcome for VC-backed startups.** IPOs are rare (hundreds per year globally). Most exits are acquisitions, and most acquisitions are for talent or technology (acqui-hires), not revenue.

**Types of acquisitions:**

| Type | Buyer Motivation | Typical Price | Integration |
|------|------------------|---------------|-------------|
| **Acqui-hire** | Team talent | $1-5M per engineer | Team absorbed |
| **Technology acquisition** | IP, codebase, data | $5-50M | Tech integrated |
| **Product acquisition** | Product + customer base | $20-200M | Product continued or merged |
| **Strategic acquisition** | Business + market position | $50M-$1B+ | Company operated independently |
| **Platform acquisition** | Entering a new market | $100M-$10B+ | Full integration |

### M&A Process

The M&A process has distinct phases, each requiring different skills and focus.

**Phase 1: Preparation (ongoing)**

Before you're approached, be ready.

| Item | Why It Matters |
|------|----------------|
| **Clean cap table** | Simple equity structure, no surprises |
| **Audited financials** | 2-3 years of GAAP audited statements |
| **IP ownership** | All IP assigned to the company, no encumbrances |
| **Key employee retention** | Critical people are locked in with retention packages |
| **Customer concentration** | No single customer > 20% of revenue |
| **Data room** | Organized repository of legal, financial, operational docs |

**Phase 2: Outreach / Inbound (weeks to months)**

M&A usually starts one of two ways:

- **Inbound approach** — a larger company reaches out. This happens when you're winning in a space they care about.
- **Process** — you hire an investment banker to run an auction. This maximizes price but takes 6-12 months and is public.

**Inbound approach playbook:**

1. Take the meeting but don't reveal urgency
2. Understand their motivation: what do they want? (team, tech, customers, revenue?)
3. Don't name a price first — let them anchor
4. Keep the process confidential
5. Bring in experienced counsel (M&A lawyer)

**Phase 3: LOI (Letter of Intent) (1-2 weeks)**

The LOI is a non-binding document that outlines key terms. It sets the framework for negotiation.

**Key LOI terms:**

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Purchase price** | Total consideration (cash + stock + earnout) |
| **Structure** | Asset sale vs stock sale (tax implications differ) |
| **Earnout** | Additional payment based on future performance |
| **Exclusivity** | You can't shop the deal to others (30-90 days) |
| **Due diligence** | What they'll review and timeline |
| **Representations and warranties** | Your promises about the company |
| **Key employee retention** | Who must stay and for how long |

**Phase 4: Due Diligence (30-90 days)**

The acquirer examines every aspect of your business. Be prepared for intense scrutiny.

| Area | What They Review |
|------|------------------|
| **Financial** | Revenue recognition, contracts, AR/AP, tax returns |
| **Legal** | IP ownership, employment agreements, litigation history |
| **Product** | Architecture, code quality, security posture, tech debt |
| **Customer** | Top customers, churn, satisfaction, contracts |
| **Team** | Org chart, key person dependency, retention risk |
| **Market** | TAM, competitive position, growth trajectory |

**Due diligence best practices:**

- Designate a single point of contact for data requests
- Organize everything in a virtual data room before the process starts
- Be honest about problems — discovered issues are fixable, hidden issues are deal-breakers
- Keep the business running — don't let diligence distract from operations

**Phase 5: Definitive Agreement (2-4 weeks)**

Lawyers draft the final purchase agreement. Key terms from the LOI are finalized and expanded.

**Phase 6: Integration Planning (1-3 months before close)**

Plan how the company will be absorbed. This is often where value is lost or preserved.

- **Team integration** — who reports to whom, where do they sit, what's the culture plan?
- **Product integration** — will the product continue, merge, or be sunset?
- **Customer integration** — how are customers transitioned, supported, and upsold?
- **Technology integration** — infrastructure, data, security

**Phase 7: Close**

Money transfers. Ownership changes. The deal is done.

**Post-close considerations:**

- Retention packages typically pay out over 1-4 years
- Earnout payments depend on hitting agreed targets
- Non-compete clauses restrict what you can do next
- Your role in the acquiring company (stay or leave?)

### Acquisition Offer Evaluation

Not all acquisition offers are good. Evaluating an offer requires understanding the full picture.

**Components of an offer:**

| Component | What It Means | How to Evaluate |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Cash at close** | Money you receive immediately | Most certain, least risk |
| **Stock in acquirer** | Equity in the buying company | Depends on acquirer's stock value, liquidity |
| **Earnout** | Future payment based on performance | Risk of non-payment, requires ongoing involvement |
| **Retention package** | Additional comp for staying | Typically 1-4 year vest |
| **Assumed options** | Employee options converted | Employees care about this |

**The earnout trap:**

Earnouts sound good but are risky. After acquisition, you lose control over resources, strategy, and priorities. Earning the earnout requires hitting targets without the autonomy to make it happen. Most earnouts don't pay in full.

**Evaluating an offer:**

| Factor | Questions to Ask |
|--------|------------------|
| **Price** | Is the total consideration fair? How does it compare to alternatives? |
| **Structure** | How much is cash vs stock vs earnout? |
| **Risk** | What could go wrong between signing and close? |
| **Timing** | When do I get paid? Over what period? |
| **Post-close** | What's my role? Do I want to stay? |
| **Team** | What happens to my employees? |
| **Customers** | What happens to the product? Will customers be served? |

**Comparing to alternatives:**

```
Offer A: $50M all cash
  - Certainty: High (cash at close)
  - Upside: None
  - Risk: None

Offer B: $30M cash + $30M earnout (total $60M)
  - Certainty: Medium (cash portion)
  - Upside: $10M more if earnout hits
  - Risk: Earnout may not pay

Alternative: Raise Series B at $100M valuation
  - Certainty: Low (fundraising may fail)
  - Upside: Potentially much higher
  - Risk: Company may fail, dilution
```

**When to walk away:**

- The price doesn't reflect the value you've built
- The acquirer's culture would destroy the product or team
- Retention packages don't adequately protect your team
- The deal structure is too risky (heavy earnout, illiquid stock)
- Your gut says no

### IPO Readiness

An IPO is the gold standard of exits but requires exceptional preparation.

**IPO prerequisites:**

| Requirement | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Revenue scale** | Typically $100M+ ARR for tech IPOs |
| **Growth rate** | 20%+ year-over-year |
| **Market size** | Large TAM ($1B+) |
| **Predictability** | Consistent financial performance |
| **Controls** | Sarbanes-Oxley financial controls |
| **Board** | Independent directors, committees |
| **Auditor** | Big Four auditing firm |
| **Compliance** | SEC reporting readiness |

**The IPO process timeline:**

```
Month 1-2: Select underwriters (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, etc.)
Month 3-4: Prepare S-1 registration statement
Month 5-6: SEC review and comments
Month 7-8: Update S-1, prepare roadshow
Month 9: Roadshow (2 weeks of investor meetings)
Month 10: Price and trade
```

**Cost of going public:**

| Cost | Estimated Amount |
|------|------------------|
| Underwriting fees | 3-7% of offering amount |
| Legal fees | $1-3M |
| Accounting fees | $500K-2M |
| Printing and filing | $100-300K |
| Insurance (D&O) | $500K-2M/year ongoing |
| Compliance costs | $1-3M/year ongoing |

**Lockup period:**

After IPO, insiders (founders, employees, early investors) typically cannot sell shares for 180 days. After the lockup expires, you can sell gradually.

**Is IPO right for you?**

| IPO Upsides | IPO Downsides |
|-------------|---------------|
| Maximum liquidity for all shareholders | Intense quarterly scrutiny |
| Currency for acquisitions | Public disclosure of everything |
| Brand and credibility | Regulatory and compliance burden |
| Ability to raise more capital | Short-term market pressure |
| Liquidity for employees | Loss of control and privacy |

### Secondary Transactions

Secondary sales allow founders and employees to sell shares before an exit. They're increasingly common.

**Types of secondary transactions:**

| Type | How It Works | Best For |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Tender offer** | Company facilitates share purchase by new investors | Employee liquidity |
| **Founder sale** | Founder sells personal shares | Founder diversification |
| **Employee liquidity program** | Regular share buybacks | Employee retention |
| **Secondary round** | New investors buy existing shares | Price discovery, liquidity |
| **SPV purchase** | Special purpose vehicle buys shares | Targeted liquidity |

**When to consider secondary sales:**

- You've been building for 7+ years with no exit in sight
- You have significant personal wealth tied up in the company
- Employees are asking for liquidity
- The company is profitable and growing but not IPO-ready
- You want to de-risk your personal financial situation

**Secondary sale considerations:**

- **Valuation** — secondary sales often happen at a discount to the primary valuation
- **Approval** — the board must approve; investors may have right of first refusal
- **Tax** — selling shares triggers capital gains tax
- **Signaling** — a large secondary sale can signal lack of confidence if not handled properly
- **Employee morale** — offering liquidity to employees can improve retention

### Liquidation Waterfall

The liquidation waterfall shows who gets paid and in what order when a company is sold.

**Standard waterfall:**

```
1. Senior debt (bank loans, venture debt)
2. Liquidation preference to preferred shareholders (investors)
3. Participation (if applicable)
4. Common shareholders (founders, employees)
```

**Liquidation preference examples:**

| Structure | Investor Gets | Common Gets |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| **1x non-participating** | Investment back first, then share remaining pro-rata | Pro-rata share of remaining |
| **1x participating** | Investment back first, then share remaining pro-rata with common | Pro-rata share after preference |
| **2x participating** | 2x investment back first, then share remaining | Pro-rata share after preference |

**Example: Company sells for $100M**

Investors put in $30M at 1x non-participating. Founders/employees own 50% of common.

```
1. Investors get $30M (preference)
2. Remaining $70M split pro-rata:
   - Investors (50%): $35M
   - Common (50%): $35M
3. Total to investors: $30M + $35M = $65M
4. Total to common: $35M
```

Same scenario with 1x participating:

```
1. Investors get $30M (preference)
2. Remaining $70M split:
   - Investors participate as common: $35M
   - Common: $35M
3. Total to investors: $30M + $35M = $65M
4. Total to common: $35M
```

In this case the math is the same because investors also own 50% of common. But if investors owned 30% of common:

With non-participating:

```
1. Investors: $30M preference + 30% of $70M = $30M + $21M = $51M
2. Common (70%): $49M
```

With participating:

```
1. Investors: $30M + $21M = $51M
2. Common: $49M
```

Same because the preference was already covered. The difference matters when the exit is closer to the investment amount.

**Sale for $40M with $30M invested:**

Non-participating:
```
1. Investors: $30M (preference takes most of $40M)
2. Common: $10M
```

Participating:
```
1. Investors: $30M + participating share of $10M ($30M × 30%) = $30M + $3M = $33M
2. Common: $7M
```

**The impact on founder motivation:**

If investors have a 2x participating preference and the company sells for a modest amount, common shareholders (founders, employees) get nothing. Understanding this helps founders negotiate fair terms and work toward exits that generate returns for everyone.

### Shutting Down

Not all companies succeed. Shutting down gracefully is a skill every founder should understand.

**When to shut down:**

- You've run out of viable funding options
- The market has moved away from your product
- The team is no longer motivated or able to execute
- The unit economics don't work and can't be fixed
- The founder has lost conviction

**The shutdown process:**

1. **Board approval.** Formally inform the board. They need to approve the dissolution.
2. **Legal counsel.** Hire a lawyer experienced in dissolution. State laws vary.
3. **Notifying employees.** Be honest, respectful, and clear about severance and benefits.
4. **Customer communication.** Give customers time to export data and transition.
5. **Vendor and partner notification.** Cancel contracts, pay outstanding invoices.
6. **Asset liquidation.** Sell IP, equipment, or data if possible.
7. **Investor communication.** Return remaining capital per liquidation preference.
8. **Dissolution.** File articles of dissolution with the state.

**The shutdown checklist:**

| Task | Timeline | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Board vote | Week 1 | Formal resolution |
| Employee notice | Week 1 | Severance, benefits continuation |
| Customer data export | Week 1-2 | Give 30-90 days for migration |
| Vendor cancellations | Week 1-2 | Check contract terms |
| Payroll and taxes | Week 1 | Final paychecks, tax filings |
| Asset sales | Week 2-4 | IP, equipment, customer lists |
| Final tax return | Year-end | Mark "final return" |
| Articles of dissolution | After everything | Official dissolution |

**Founder emotional preparation:**

Shutting down is emotionally devastating. You've invested years of your life. It's normal to grieve.

- Allow yourself to feel the loss
- Talk to other founders who've shut down — you're not alone
- Take time off before starting anything new
- Reflect on what you learned, not what you lost
- Investors who've been through this will respect your honesty

### Common Mistakes

**Not preparing for exit from day one.** Clean cap table, proper IP assignments, auditable books. These take years to establish and acquirers check them all.

**Taking the first offer.** If you haven't shopped the deal or gotten competing interest, you're leaving money on the table. At minimum, let 2-3 potential acquirers know you're exploring options.

**Falling in love with the acquirer.** A big company showing interest feels validating. Don't let the attention cloud your judgment about the deal terms.

**Ignoring earnout risk.** Earnouts are risky. Negotiate for more cash at close and less earnout.

**Not protecting the team.** The acquisition price is great for you, but your employees may not get the same benefit. Negotiate for retention packages that protect your team.

**Staying too long post-acquisition.** If your earnout requires 3 years of employment, be sure you want to work at the acquiring company for 3 years. Most founders leave within 12-18 months.

**Shutting down too late (or too early).** Shutting down too late burns through remaining capital and stresses the team. Shutting down too early may miss a recovery. Get board and investor input to calibrate the timing.

**Going public too early.** An IPO before you have predictable financials, a large market, and internal controls is a disaster. Your stock will tank, you'll be stuck with public company costs and scrutiny, and fundraising becomes harder.

## Study Cases

### Case 1: The Earnout That Didn't Pay

A founder sold his company for $40M — $20M cash at close and $20M earnout based on 2 years of revenue targets. After acquisition, the acquirer redirected the product team, changed the pricing model, and cut the marketing budget. Revenue dropped. The earnout paid $5M instead of $20M. The founder felt trapped — he couldn't control the outcomes that determined his pay.

Lesson: Earnouts are a bet on the acquirer's behavior. Negotiate for more cash and shorter earnout periods.

### Case 2: The Graceful Shutdown

A B2B SaaS startup after 5 years had great technology but couldn't find product-market fit. The founders had 3 months of runway left. Instead of burning through it, they called a board meeting, presented the data, and agreed to shut down. They gave customers 60 days to export data, helped them migrate to competitors, and paid all vendors. The IP was sold to a larger company for $500K, which went to note holders. Employees got 2 months severance and glowing references.

Lesson: A graceful shutdown preserves relationships, reputation, and self-respect. It's the right call when the data says it's time.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Earnout | Additional purchase price paid based on post-acquisition performance |
| Liquidation preference | The order and amount investors get paid before common shareholders |
| LOI | Letter of Intent — non-binding document outlining acquisition terms |
| Lockup period | Time after IPO during which insiders cannot sell shares |
| Secondary sale | Sale of existing shares (not new shares issued) |
| Acqui-hire | Acquisition primarily for the team, not the product |
| Due diligence | The acquirer's investigation of the target company |
| S-1 | Registration statement filed with SEC for an IPO |
| Waterfall | The order of payment distribution in an exit |
| Tender offer | A company-facilitated program for employees to sell shares |
