### The Founding Team

The ideal founding team has complementary skills. The most common pattern is a builder (CTO) and a seller (CEO). A third co-founder who owns operations or product can complete the triangle, but two is often enough.

**Co-founder selection is the most important decision you'll make.** It's more important than your product idea. You'll spend more time with this person than your family for years.

**What to look for:**
- Shared values and work ethic
- Complementary skills (not overlapping)
- Trust — tested through working together before committing
- Conflict resolution style — can you disagree productively?
- Long-term alignment — same ambition level, same risk tolerance

**Co-founder agreement.** Document everything early:
- Equity split and vesting schedule
- Roles and responsibilities
- Decision-making process
- What happens if someone leaves
- Intellectual property assignment

### First Hires

Your first 10 employees define the company culture. Hire carefully.

**Who to hire first:**

| Role | When | Why |
|------|------|-----|
| **Engineer** | Day 1 | You need to build the product |
| **Designer** | Early product stage | Design is competitive advantage |
| **Customer support** | After first paying customers | Retaining customers is critical |
| **Sales / BD** | After product-market fit | Scale what's working |
| **Marketing** | After repeatable revenue | Amplify proven channels |
| **Operations / Finance** | 10–20 employees | Processes start to matter |

**What to look for in early hires:**

- **Generalists** — they'll wear many hats, especially at startups
- **High ownership** — they care about outcomes, not just tasks
- **Learning velocity** — they figure things out fast
- **Low ego** — they'll do what needs doing, even if it's "not their job"
- **Belief in the mission** — they'd join even at a salary discount

**Hiring process.** Standardize early:

1. **Screening call** — values, motivation, communication
2. **Technical assessment** — relevant to actual work (not whiteboard trivia)
3. **Team interview** — cross-functional, culture fit
4. **Founder interview** — mission alignment, final decision
5. **Reference checks** — talk to past managers and peers
6. **Offer** — verbal → written, 3–5 day deadline

### Company Culture

**Culture is what you tolerate.** Not what you write on a wall. It's how decisions are made, how mistakes are handled, and what happens when someone underperforms.

**Define your values early.** 3–5 values that guide decisions:

| Value | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Bias for action** | Move fast, learn, iterate |
| **Radical transparency** | Share information, even when it's uncomfortable |
| **Customer obsession** | Every decision starts with the customer |
| **Ownership** | Take responsibility for outcomes, not inputs |
| **High agency** | Don't wait for permission; fix problems |

**Culture in practice:**

- How are disagreements resolved? (argue openly, decide, commit)
- How is failure handled? (blameless post-mortems vs blame)
- How are decisions made? (founder decides vs consensus vs delegated)
- What gets celebrated? (shipping vs hours worked vs revenue milestones)

### Compensation & Equity

Early-stage companies can't match big tech salaries. Compensate with equity and mission.

**Cash compensation benchmarks:**

| Stage | Engineer Salary | % of Market Rate |
|-------|----------------|------------------|
| Pre-seed / Seed | $60k–$100k | 60–80% |
| Series A | $100k–$140k | 80–90% |
| Series B+ | $140k–$180k | 90–100% |

**Equity grants (for US startups):**

| Role | Typical Grant |
|------|---------------|
| Co-founder | 20–50% (split among founders) |
| Early engineer (#1–5) | 0.5–3% |
| Early engineer (#5–15) | 0.2–1% |
| VP / Director | 0.5–1.5% |
| Early non-engineer | 0.2–0.5% |

**Refresh grants.** Ongoing equity grants to retain employees after their initial grant vests. Typically annual or promotion-based.

### Remote & Distributed Teams

Decide early whether your company is remote-first, office-first, or hybrid. Changing later is painful.

**Remote-first** — design everything for async communication:
- Documentation over meetings
- Written decisions with clear rationale
- Flexible schedules with overlap hours
- Regular async check-ins
- Annual or semi-annual in-person gatherings

**Office-first** — colocation enables faster feedback:
- Faster onboarding
- Richer collaboration
- Stronger social bonds
- Better for early-stage product development

**Hybrid** — the hardest to get right. Remote employees often feel like second-class citizens. If hybrid, invest in tools and practices that include remote participants equally.

### Scaling the Team

| Stage | Team Size | Key Challenge |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| **Seed** | 2–10 | Finding product-market fit |
| **Series A** | 10–30 | Hiring first managers |
| **Series B** | 30–80 | Building processes without bureaucracy |
| **Series C+** | 80–300+ | Maintaining culture, managing managers |

**As you grow:**
- **Communication** — what worked in Slack at 5 people is chaos at 50
- **Delegation** — founders can't make every decision
- **Process** — some process is good; too much kills velocity
- **Culture** — new employees dilute culture; reinforce it deliberately
- **DEI** — diversity and inclusion need intentional investment

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Equity grant | Shares or options given to employees as compensation |
| Vesting | Earning equity over time (typically 4 years) |
| Refresh grant | Additional equity grant after initial grant vests |
| Remote-first | Company designed for asynchronous, distributed work |
| Culture | Shared values, behaviors, and norms that define how work gets done |
