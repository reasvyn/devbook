## Content / Material

### Funding Sources

| Source | Amount | Dilution | Best For |
|--------|--------|----------|----------|
| **Bootstrapping** | Your revenue | None | Sustainable businesses, proven models |
| **Friends & Family** | $10k–$100k | Usually equity or convertible note | Very early stage |
| **Grants** | $50k–$500k | None | R&D, hard tech (SBIR, NSF) |
| **Accelerators** | $100k–$500k | 5–10% | Early validation, network, mentorship |
| **Angel Investors** | $25k–$500k | 10–20% | Pre-seed and seed stage |
| **Venture Capital** | $500k–$100M+ | 15–30% per round | High-growth scalable businesses |
| **Revenue (Customers)** | Variable | None | The best source — proves demand |

**Bootstrapping first is always a good idea.** Revenue is the best fundraising — it proves people want your product. Raise external capital when you need to accelerate something that's already working, not to figure out if it works.

### Fundraising Stages

| Stage | Typical Raise | Typical Valuation | Key Milestone |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Pre-seed** | $100k–$1M | $2M–$8M | Team + prototype + early traction |
| **Seed** | $1M–$5M | $8M–$20M | Product-market fit signal |
| **Series A** | $5M–$15M | $20M–$60M | Repeatable growth model |
| **Series B** | $15M–$50M | $60M–$200M | Scaling proven business |
| **Series C+** | $50M+ | $200M+ | Market dominance, expansion |

Each stage has different expectations. At pre-seed, investors bet on the team. At Series A, they bet on traction. At Series B+, they bet on metrics and market size.

### Valuation

Valuation is part art, part science. Key factors:

- **Traction** — revenue, growth rate, user engagement
- **Market size** — TAM (Total Addressable Market)
- **Team** — founder background, domain expertise
- **Comparables** — what similar companies raised at similar stages
- **Demand** — how many investors want to invest

**Pre-money vs post-money valuation:**

```
Pre-money valuation = value of company before investment
Investment amount = cash coming in
Post-money valuation = pre-money + investment

Example: $5M pre-money + $2M investment = $7M post-money
Investor owns: $2M / $7M = 28.6%
```

**Methods:**

- **Comparable company analysis** — what are similar startups worth?
- **Scorecard method** — evaluate team, product, market, competition, channel
- **VC method** — work backward from expected exit value
- **Berkshire method** — what would a rational buyer pay for the cash flows?

### Term Sheet Terms

A term sheet is a non-binding document outlining investment terms. Key terms:

| Term | What It Means |
|------|---------------|
| **Valuation** | Pre-money and post-money |
| **Liquidation preference** | Investor gets paid first (1× is standard) |
| **Participation** | Does investor get their preference AND share remaining proceeds? |
| **Board composition** | How many seats, who controls |
| **Pro-rata rights** | Right to invest in future rounds |
| **Anti-dilution** | Protection if later rounds are at lower price (weighted average is standard) |
| **Information rights** | What financials you must share |
| **Drag-along** | Majority shareholders can force minority to accept an acquisition |
| **No-shop clause** | You can't shop the deal to other investors for 30–60 days |

**Liquidation preference example:** Investor puts in $5M at a 1× preference. You sell the company for $15M. They get their $5M back first, then the remaining $10M is split pro-rata. If preference is 2× participating, they get $10M then split the remaining $5M.

Most standard term sheets from top firms are fair. Focus on valuation, board control, and liquidation preference. The rest is usually standard.

### The Fundraising Process

1. **Prepare** — build your data room (pitch deck, financial model, cap table, customer data)
2. **Warm outreach** — use your network for introductions (cold emails rarely work)
3. **First meeting** — 30-minute pitch, focus on problem, traction, and vision
4. **Follow-up** — share additional materials, answer questions
5. **Due diligence** — investors review your legal, financial, and technical details
6. **Term sheet** — negotiate key terms
7. **Legal process** — lawyers draft definitive agreements
8. **Close** — money transfers to your bank account

**Timeline.** Plan 3–6 months from start to close. Have 3–6 months of runway before you start so you're not desperate.

### Investor Relationships

**Find the right investors.** Look for investors who:
- Understand your space
- Have a track record of helping portfolio companies
- Are helpful but not intrusive
- Have relevant network and expertise

**What investors look for:**

- **Founder-market fit** — why are you the right person to solve this problem?
- **Clarity** — can you explain your business simply?
- **Coachability** — do you listen and adapt?
- **Resilience** — have you faced and overcome challenges?
- **Integrity** — do you tell the truth, especially when it's hard?

**After the investment.** Investors are your partners. Communicate regularly (monthly updates). Be transparent about problems — bad news doesn't get better with time. Ask for help when you need it: introductions, advice, recruiting support.
