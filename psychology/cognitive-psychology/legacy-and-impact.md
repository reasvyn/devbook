## Content / Material

### Beyond Personal Achievement

The first three documents of this module focus on the personal dimension of purpose — finding your why, building a vision, and setting goals. These are essential. Without them, you have no foundation for contribution. But they are not the end of the journey. The end is what you leave behind.

Personal achievement is about what you accomplish in your lifetime. Legacy is about what persists after you are gone. The two are related but distinct. You can achieve extraordinary things and leave nothing behind. You can achieve modest things and leave an extraordinary legacy. The difference is intentionality.

The shift from achievement to legacy is a shift in time horizon. Achievement looks at the next quarter, the next year, the next five years. Legacy looks at the next generation, the next century. The shift is not about denying the importance of personal achievement. It is about contextualizing it. Achievement is the means. Legacy is the end.

The shift also requires a shift in identity. You stop thinking of yourself as someone who achieves and start thinking of yourself as someone who contributes. The contribution identity is more sustainable because it is not dependent on external recognition. You can contribute quietly, without applause, and the contribution still counts.

### The Seven Generations Principle

The seven generations principle comes from the Iroquois Confederacy, also known as the Haudenosaunee. The principle states that decisions should be made with consideration of their impact on the seventh generation yet to come. Every decision, every action, every policy should be evaluated by how it will affect people living seven generations from now.

The seven generations principle is a framework for long-term thinking that extends far beyond typical planning horizons. Seven generations is roughly 140 to 210 years. This is not a metaphorical timeframe. It is a concrete decision-making criterion. Will this decision benefit people who will be born two centuries from now?

The principle has profound implications for how you think about your work, your consumption, your investments, and your relationships. Most decisions are made with a horizon of months or years. The seven generations principle asks you to extend that horizon to centuries. The extension changes the calculus. Short-term gains that produce long-term harm are revealed as unacceptable. Short-term costs that produce long-term benefits become obvious investments.

The seven generations principle also has implications for purpose. Your purpose is not just about your lifetime. It is about the trajectory you set in motion. The work you do today can create conditions for flourishing that extend far beyond your own life. The seven generations perspective gives your purpose a time horizon that matches its significance.

### Applying the Seven Generations Principle Today

Applying the seven generations principle in daily life requires a shift in how you evaluate decisions. The first step is to extend your time horizon. Before making a significant decision, ask: how will this affect people seven generations from now? The question does not always produce a clear answer, but the act of asking shifts your perspective.

The second step is to evaluate the sustainability of your actions. Are you consuming resources that future generations will need? Are you building systems that can persist without your ongoing involvement? The sustainability criterion is the practical application of the seven generations principle.

The third step is to invest in institutions that outlast individuals. Libraries, schools, parks, community organizations, open source projects — these are the vehicles through which impact extends beyond a single lifetime. Your investment in these institutions pays dividends for generations.

The fourth step is to transfer knowledge and skills. The seven generations principle is not just about physical resources. It is about cultural resources. The knowledge, skills, values, and practices you pass on are the most enduring form of legacy. They persist through generations because they are carried in minds and habits.

### Building vs Consuming

The distinction between building and consuming is central to legacy. Building creates something that persists. Consuming uses something that exists. Both are necessary. You must consume to survive. But the balance between building and consuming determines the direction of your life.

Building takes many forms. Building a business, a relationship, a skill, a body of work, a community, a family. The common element is creation. You are bringing something into existence that did not exist before. The created thing may be tangible or intangible. It may be a product, a service, a relationship, or a piece of knowledge. The creation is the essence.

Consuming also takes many forms. Consuming food, entertainment, information, products, experiences. The common element is use. You are using something that already exists. The consumption may be necessary or optional. The consumption may be enriching or depleting. The common element is that you are taking rather than giving.

The balance between building and consuming determines your legacy. A life dominated by consumption produces no legacy. The consumed resources are gone. A life dominated by building produces a legacy. The built things persist. The balance does not need to be extreme. Even a small surplus of building over consuming, sustained over a lifetime, produces a significant legacy.

### The Creator Mindset

The creator mindset is the orientation that prioritizes building over consuming. It is not about never consuming. It is about being aware of the balance and actively choosing to create more than you consume.

The creator mindset begins with the question: what can I make today? The question can be applied to any domain. What can I make at work? A better process, a stronger team, a more useful product. What can I make at home? A healthier meal, a more organized space, a warmer relationship. What can I make in the world? A piece of writing, a piece of art, a piece of code, a piece of community.

The creator mindset is also about ownership. Creators own their output. They take responsibility for what they produce. The ownership creates a different relationship to the work. When you own what you create, you care about its quality. You want it to last. You want it to serve.

The creator mindset is cultivated through practice. You start by creating something small. A paragraph. A sketch. A recipe. A garden bed. The small creation builds the identity of a creator. The identity expands to larger creations over time.

### The Consumer Trap

The consumer trap is the default state of modern life. The economy is designed to turn you into a consumer. Advertising, media, social platforms, and retail environments all push toward consumption. The default is to consume. The conscious choice is to create.

The consumer trap is seductive because consumption is easy. Creation is hard. Consumption provides immediate pleasure. Creation provides delayed satisfaction. Consumption requires no skill. Creation requires practice. The asymmetry means that without conscious effort, your life will tip toward consumption.

The consumer trap is also isolating. Consumption is a solitary activity. Even when done in groups, consumption creates passive shared experience rather than active connection. Creation connects you to others through shared purpose. The builder and the user have a relationship. The consumer and the consumed have none.

The escape from the consumer trap is not abstinence. It is balance. The goal is not to stop consuming. The goal is to consume intentionally and create consistently. The ratio matters more than the absolute level.

### From Consumer to Creator

The transition from consumer to creator is a shift in identity and behavior. The shift happens in stages.

Stage one is awareness. You recognize the imbalance between consumption and creation in your life. You see how much time you spend consuming and how little creating. The awareness is uncomfortable but necessary.

Stage two is reduction. You deliberately reduce consumption. You set limits on social media, entertainment, and passive information intake. The reduction frees time and attention for creation.

Stage three is experimentation. You try creating in small ways. A journal entry. A photograph. A comment that adds value. A small fix to an open source project. The experiments are low risk. They build the creator identity without requiring a major commitment.

Stage four is consistency. You establish a regular creation practice. The practice may be daily or weekly. The key is consistency. The consistent practice builds skills, confidence, and output.

Stage five is contribution. Your creation is now serving others. People use what you make. The contribution is the fulfillment of the creator identity. You are not just creating for yourself. You are creating for the world.

### Open Source and Digital Legacy

Open source software is software whose source code is publicly available for anyone to use, modify, and distribute. The open source movement is built on the principles of transparency, collaboration, and contribution. It is one of the most powerful models for digital legacy.

An open source contribution can outlive its creator by decades. Code written today can be running on servers fifty years from now. The contribution is persistent because it is embedded in systems that outlast individuals. The open source project continues after the original contributor is gone.

Open source is also a force multiplier. A single contribution can be used by thousands or millions of people. The contribution is amplified by the network. The impact of the contribution is not limited by the contributor's direct reach. It scales through adoption.

Open source is not limited to software. The principles apply to any domain where knowledge can be shared freely. Open educational resources, open science, open data, open design. Any contribution to the open commons is a form of digital legacy.

### Why Digital Legacy Matters

Digital legacy matters because the digital domain is where an increasing portion of human activity takes place. The knowledge, tools, and infrastructure of the digital world shape how future generations live, work, and think. Contributing to the digital commons is contributing to the foundation of future civilization.

Digital legacy also matters because it is accessible. You do not need wealth, status, or institutional affiliation to create a digital legacy. Anyone with internet access can contribute. The barriers to entry are lower than almost any other form of legacy creation.

Digital legacy matters because it is persistent. Physical artifacts decay. Digital artifacts can persist indefinitely if they are maintained. A well-maintained open source project, a comprehensive documentation site, or a collection of educational resources can serve generations.

Digital legacy matters because it compounds. A contribution that helps one person can be adapted to help many. Each adaptation extends the reach of the original contribution. The compounding is exponential.

### Forms of Digital Legacy

Code is the most obvious form of digital legacy for developers. An open source library, a tool, a framework, or a well-documented project. The code is used, forked, and built upon by others.

Writing is a form of digital legacy available to everyone. Blog posts, tutorials, documentation, essays, books. Writing persists because it can be copied, stored, and retrieved indefinitely. The written word is the oldest form of digital legacy.

Educational content is a particularly powerful form of legacy. A well-crafted tutorial or course can teach thousands of people over many years. The educational content creates a chain of learning that extends beyond the original teacher.

Community building is a form of legacy that creates ongoing value. A forum, a discussion group, a meetup, or an online community can continue after the founder is no longer involved. The community becomes self-sustaining.

### Mentoring and Teaching as a Force Multiplier

Mentoring is the practice of sharing knowledge, experience, and guidance with someone less experienced. Teaching is the formalized version of the same practice. Both are force multipliers because they create new contributors.

A single mentor can influence dozens of mentees over a career. Each mentee, in turn, influences others. The influence multiplies across generations. The mentor's impact extends far beyond what they could achieve alone.

Teaching has the same multiplier effect. A good teacher can transform how hundreds or thousands of students think about a domain. The students carry the teacher's ideas forward. The ideas spread through the students' own work and teaching.

The multiplier effect of mentoring and teaching is the most reliable form of legacy. Unlike code or writing, which can become obsolete, the ideas and skills passed to another person can be adapted to changing circumstances. The human transmission of knowledge is resilient.

### The Multiplier Effect

The math of the multiplier effect is compelling. If you mentor one person per year for thirty years, and each of those people goes on to mentor one person per year, your indirect mentees grow exponentially. In the first generation, you have thirty direct mentees. In the second generation, assuming each mentee mentors one person per year for five years, you have 150 indirect mentees. In the third generation, the number grows further.

The actual numbers depend on the specifics of the mentoring relationships, but the principle holds. Mentoring creates a legacy that grows beyond your direct contribution. The growth is not linear. It is exponential.

The multiplier effect also applies to teaching. A single lecture can change how an entire cohort thinks. The changed thinking influences the work they do for the rest of their careers. The lecture is a single event. Its impact extends across decades through the work of the students.

The multiplier effect is why investing in people is the highest-leverage form of legacy work. The return on investment in a person is not limited by your capacity. Once the person is developed, they create value independently.

### Forms of Mentoring

Formal mentoring is structured. You are assigned a mentee or you volunteer for a program. The relationship has clear expectations, regular meetings, and defined goals. Formal mentoring is reliable but can feel mechanical.

Informal mentoring is organic. You notice someone who could benefit from your experience and you offer guidance. The relationship develops naturally. Informal mentoring is more flexible but less reliable.

Peer mentoring is mutual. You and a peer exchange guidance and support. Each person brings different strengths. The relationship is equal rather than hierarchical. Peer mentoring is sustainable because both parties benefit.

Group mentoring extends your reach. You mentor a small group rather than an individual. The group provides peer support among its members. Group mentoring is more efficient in terms of time investment per person reached.

Remote mentoring extends your geographic reach. You mentor people who are not in your physical location. Remote mentoring is enabled by technology and can reach people who would not otherwise have access to mentors.

### The Mentorship Mindset

The mentorship mindset is the orientation that sees every interaction as a potential teaching moment. You do not need a formal mentorship relationship to practice the mindset. You can mentor informally in conversations, meetings, and collaborations.

The mentorship mindset begins with generosity. You share what you know without expecting immediate return. The generosity is not naive. It recognizes that knowledge shared is knowledge multiplied. The return comes indirectly through the network effects of contribution.

The mentorship mindset also requires humility. You recognize that you do not have all the answers. The mentor is not the expert who dispenses wisdom. The mentor is the guide who helps the mentee discover their own answers. The humility makes the mentoring relationship collaborative rather than hierarchical.

The mentorship mindset is sustained by the satisfaction of seeing others grow. The growth of a mentee is a tangible legacy. You can see the impact of your guidance in the decisions they make and the contributions they produce.

### Writing and Creating

Writing is the most accessible form of legacy creation. You do not need special equipment, credentials, or permission. You need a topic, a willingness to share, and consistency. The barrier to entry is almost zero.

Writing creates legacy because it persists. A piece of writing can be read years or decades after it was written. It can be shared, copied, and referenced. The writing continues to serve readers long after the writer is gone.

Writing also creates legacy because it clarifies thinking. The process of writing forces you to organize your thoughts, test your assumptions, and articulate your understanding clearly. The clarity benefits you in the moment and creates a record that benefits others later.

Writing compounds. Each piece of writing builds on previous pieces. The body of work grows. The accumulated writing becomes a resource that is more valuable than any single piece. The body of work is the legacy.

### Why Writing Is a Legacy Lever

Writing is a legacy lever because it scales. A single piece of writing can reach thousands or millions of people. The reach is not limited by your time or energy. Once written, the piece continues to work without additional effort.

Writing is a legacy lever because it is searchable. People searching for answers to specific questions can find your writing years after it was published. The searchability extends the useful life of the writing far beyond the initial publication.

Writing is a legacy lever because it is remixable. Other people can build on your writing, adapt it, combine it with other ideas, and create new work. The remixing extends the reach and impact of the original.

Writing is a legacy lever because it is authoritative. Written work carries weight. A well-researched, well-written piece is cited, referenced, and trusted. The authority accumulates over time as the body of work grows.

### The Compound Effect of Creation

Creation compounds in two ways. First, each creation builds skills that make the next creation better. The writer improves with each piece. The developer improves with each project. The quality of the work increases over time.

Second, each creation builds an audience. The first piece of writing may have zero readers. The tenth may have ten. The hundredth may have a thousand. The audience grows as the body of work grows. The growth is nonlinear.

The compound effect of creation means that early work is an investment. The first pieces of writing, the first open source contributions, the first mentoring relationships may produce little visible impact. But they are building the foundation for later impact. The foundation must be laid before the compound effect can begin.

The compound effect also means that consistency matters more than intensity. A small creation made regularly beats a large creation made rarely. The regular practice builds the skills and the audience simultaneously.

### The Ripple Effect

The ripple effect is the phenomenon where a single action produces a chain of consequences that extend far beyond the original action. A stone dropped in a pond creates ripples that spread across the entire surface. The stone is small. The ripples are large.

The same principle applies to legacy. A single contribution can create ripples that extend across generations. The contribution is the stone. The people it influences are the ripples. Each person influenced creates their own ripples by influencing others. The cascade continues.

The ripple effect is humbling because you cannot control where the ripples go. You can choose the stone — the contribution you make. You cannot choose the ripples or predict their full extent. The ripples are determined by the environment, the timing, and the people who receive the contribution.

The ripple effect is also encouraging because it means your impact is larger than you can see. You will never fully know the extent of your influence. The people you have helped will help others who help others. The cascade continues beyond your awareness.

### How Ripples Propagate

Ripples propagate through networks. Each person who receives your contribution is a node in a network. The node connects to other nodes. The contribution spreads through the connections. The network amplifies the contribution.

Ripples propagate through teaching. When you teach someone a skill, they can teach others. The skill spreads through a chain of teaching. Each link in the chain multiplies the original contribution.

Ripples propagate through example. When you live according to your values, others observe and may adopt those values. The example is a form of influence that does not require direct interaction. The ripple spreads through observation and imitation.

Ripples propagate through artifacts. The things you build, write, or create continue to influence people after you are gone. The artifact is a vessel for your contribution. It carries your ideas forward in time.

### The Three Orders of Impact

First order impact is the direct result of your actions. The code you wrote. The person you mentored. The meal you cooked. First order impact is visible and measurable. It is the immediate consequence of your contribution.

Second order impact is the result of the results. The code you wrote enabled someone else to build something. The person you mentored mentored someone else. The meal you cooked gave someone energy to do meaningful work. Second order impact is less visible but often larger.

Third order impact is the cultural shift. The code you wrote changed how people think about a problem. The person you mentored became a leader who influenced an entire field. The meal you cooked became a tradition that brought people together across generations. Third order impact is invisible in the moment but persistent over time.

Most legacy thinking focuses on first order impact. The most significant legacies are often the result of second and third order impacts that the original contributor never anticipated.

### The Legacy of Character

Not all legacy is about what you build. Some legacy is about who you are. The legacy of character is the way you treated people, the values you embodied, and the example you set. This legacy is often more enduring than any specific achievement.

The legacy of character is transmitted through relationships. The people who knew you carry your memory. They tell stories about you. The stories shape how others think about what is possible, what is valuable, and how to live.

The legacy of character is also transmitted through habits. The values you embodied become normalized. People who observed your integrity are more likely to act with integrity. The normalization of virtue is a subtle but powerful form of legacy.

The legacy of character does not require public recognition. It operates at the scale of family, friends, and community. The impact is local but real. The people whose lives you touched are different because of you.

### How You Make People Feel

The most enduring aspect of your legacy is not what you achieved but how you made people feel. The feeling persists in memory long after the achievement is forgotten.

People remember being seen, heard, and valued. They remember kindness, encouragement, and support. They remember the person who believed in them when no one else did. These memories shape their identity and their behavior.

The feelings you create ripple outward. The person you encouraged goes on to encourage others. The person you believed in goes on to believe in others. The kindness you showed becomes a model for how they treat others. The feeling is the seed of a legacy that grows beyond your awareness.

The implication is that how you show up in daily interactions matters. The small moments of presence, attention, and care are not trivial. They are the fabric of your legacy.

### The Quiet Legacy

The quiet legacy is the legacy that is not visible to the world but is deeply meaningful to the people who experience it. It is the legacy of the parent who raised children with love and wisdom. The legacy of the teacher who inspired a love of learning in a single student. The legacy of the friend who was present in a time of need.

The quiet legacy does not produce public recognition. It may not produce any tangible artifacts. But it is real. It persists in the lives of the people who were touched by it.

The quiet legacy is available to everyone. You do not need wealth, fame, or extraordinary achievement to create it. You need presence, care, and consistency. The quiet legacy is built through thousands of small moments of genuine connection.

The quiet legacy is also the most sustainable form of legacy because it does not depend on external conditions. It is not vulnerable to market changes, technological disruption, or cultural shifts. It exists in the hearts and minds of the people you loved.

### Legacy and Ego

Legacy work is complicated by ego. The desire for legacy can easily become the desire for fame, recognition, and immortality. The ego-driven legacy is fragile because it depends on external validation. If you are not recognized, you feel your legacy is failing.

The ego-driven legacy also distorts your actions. You pursue what will be recognized rather than what will be useful. You optimize for visibility rather than impact. The distortion reduces the actual value of your contribution.

The antidote to ego in legacy work is detachment from recognition. You do the work because it matters, not because it will be remembered. You contribute because the contribution is needed, not because it will be attributed to you.

The paradox is that detachment from recognition often leads to greater recognition. The people who are most focused on contribution rather than credit often make the most significant contributions. Their focus on the work itself produces work that is worth remembering.

### The Paradox of Legacy

The paradox of legacy is that the more you try to create a legacy, the less likely you are to create one. The focus on legacy creates anxiety that undermines the quality of your work. The anxiety comes from the awareness that you are being evaluated by a future audience that you cannot see or influence.

The alternative is to focus on contribution rather than legacy. You do the work because it matters now. You help people because they need help now. You create because creation is meaningful now. The legacy emerges as a byproduct of contribution.

The paradox is resolved by shifting from outcome focus to process focus. You do not control whether your work is remembered. You control whether your work is good. The quality of the work is within your control. The legacy is not.

### Letting Go of Legacy

The final stage of legacy work is letting go. You recognize that your legacy is not yours to control. You contribute, and then you release. The release is not abandonment. It is trust. You trust that the contribution will find its way.

Letting go of legacy is essential because attachment to legacy is attachment to an outcome you cannot control. The attachment creates suffering. The release creates freedom. You are free to contribute without attachment to how the contribution is received.

Letting go of legacy is also essential because it allows you to be present. The focus on legacy pulls you into the future. You are always thinking about how your work will be remembered. The presence is lost. Letting go returns you to the present moment where contribution happens.

The letting go is not passive. It is an active practice of releasing attachment while maintaining commitment. You are fully committed to the work. You are not attached to how the work is received. The commitment without attachment is the ideal state for legacy creation.

### Legacy and Community

Legacy is not created in isolation. It is created in community. The people around you shape what you build, how you build it, and whether it persists. Community is the context for legacy.

The relationship between legacy and community is reciprocal. Community provides the support, feedback, and collaboration that enable significant contribution. Legacy provides the sense of continuity and purpose that sustains community. The health of the community and the quality of the legacy are connected.

Building legacy through community means investing in relationships. The relationships are the infrastructure through which contribution flows. A strong relationship network amplifies every contribution. A weak network dampens it.

Community also provides the accountability that sustains legacy work. When you are part of a community that values contribution, you are more likely to contribute. The community norms reinforce the behavior. The accountability is gentle but persistent.

### Legacy and Gratitude

Gratitude is the emotional foundation of legacy work. When you recognize that you have received much, you are motivated to give much. The recognition of what you have been given creates the desire to pass it forward.

Gratitude also protects against the entitlement that can corrupt legacy work. The entitled person feels that the world owes them recognition. The grateful person recognizes that they are part of something larger. The gratitude keeps the ego in check.

The practice of gratitude in legacy work is simple. Regularly acknowledge the people who have contributed to your life. The acknowledgment is not just for their benefit. It is for yours. It reminds you that you are part of a chain of contribution that extends behind and ahead of you.

### Legacy and Courage

Legacy work requires courage. It requires the courage to create when creation is difficult, to share when sharing is vulnerable, and to persist when persistence is lonely. The courage is not the absence of fear. It is action in the presence of fear.

The courage to create legacy comes from the conviction that the work matters. The conviction does not need to be certain. It needs to be strong enough to sustain action. The courage grows as the work grows. Each act of courage makes the next act easier.

The courage to create legacy also comes from the awareness that time is limited. The awareness is not morbid. It is clarifying. The limited time makes the choice to contribute more urgent. The courage to act now comes from the recognition that now is all you have.

### The Daily Practice of Legacy

Legacy is not created in grand gestures. It is created in daily practices. The daily choice to create rather than consume. The daily choice to help rather than ignore. The daily choice to contribute rather than take.

The daily practice of legacy begins with intention. At the start of each day, set an intention to contribute. The intention can be small. Today I will listen more than I speak. Today I will help someone solve a problem. Today I will create something that did not exist before.

The daily practice continues with action. Throughout the day, look for opportunities to contribute. The opportunities are everywhere. A kind word. A helpful suggestion. A piece of work done with care. The opportunities are small, but their cumulative effect is large.

The daily practice ends with reflection. At the end of each day, review your contributions. What did you create? Who did you help? What will you do differently tomorrow? The reflection reinforces the identity of a contributor. The identity makes future contribution more likely.

### Walkthrough: Defining Your 100-Year Impact

This walkthrough guides you through defining the impact you want to have over the next 100 years. The 100-year timeframe is long enough to transcend your own lifetime but short enough to be imaginable.

### The 100-Year Impact Statement

The 100-year impact statement is a single paragraph describing the difference you want to make over the next century. It is not a prediction. It is an aspiration. It describes what you hope will be different because you lived.

The statement has four components. First, the domain of impact. What area of human activity do you want to influence? Technology, education, health, community, environment, culture, relationships.

Second, the nature of the impact. What kind of change do you want to create? Do you want to advance knowledge, build infrastructure, create beauty, foster connection, protect what matters, or inspire others?

Third, the scale of the impact. How many people do you want to reach? The scale can be specific or general. Reach may be local, national, or global. The scale is not about ego. It is about scope.

Fourth, the duration of the impact. How long do you want the impact to last? The 100-year timeframe is the default, but some impacts may be designed to last longer.

### The 100-Year Impact Workshop

Set aside three hours for this workshop. You will need your purpose statement, vision document, and a notebook.

Hour one: Reflect on your purpose and vision. Read your purpose statement and vision document. Sit with them for ten minutes. Then answer: given my purpose and vision, what impact do I most want to have? Write freely for thirty minutes. Do not edit. Do not judge. Let the ideas flow.

Hour two: Structure your impact statement. Review what you wrote in hour one. Identify the domain, nature, scale, and duration of your desired impact. Write a draft of your 100-year impact statement. It should be one paragraph. Read it aloud. Does it resonate? Revise until it does.

Hour three: Create your impact plan. What actions can you take this year to move toward your 100-year impact? What can you do this quarter? This month? This week? Write down specific actions. The actions are the bridge between your aspiration and your daily life.

Close the workshop by writing your 100-year impact statement on a card. Keep it where you will see it. Read it quarterly during your vision review. Let it guide your decisions without controlling them.

The 100-year impact statement is not a contract. It is a compass. It points direction. It does not prescribe the path. The path will emerge as you take action and learn. The compass ensures you are moving in the right direction.

## Learning Tips

Start with a single form of legacy work. Do not try to do everything at once. Pick one — mentoring, writing, open source contribution, community building — and commit to it for one year. The year of focused legacy work will teach you what sustainable contribution looks like. From that foundation, you can expand to other forms.
