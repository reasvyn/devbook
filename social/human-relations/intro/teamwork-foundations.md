### Stages of Team Development

Tuckman's model describes how teams evolve:

| Stage | What Happens | Developer Experience |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Forming** | Team meets, polite, uncertain | First standup, learning who does what |
| **Storming** | Disagreements, friction, tension | Arguments about architecture, code style, process |
| **Norming** | Norms emerge, trust builds | Team agrees on conventions, review standards |
| **Performing** | High function, flow | Ship consistently, trust each other's decisions |
| **Adjourning** | Team disbands | Project ends, team members move on |

**Most teams get stuck in Storming.** The difference between high-performing and dysfunctional teams is whether they push through conflict to Norming, or stay in perpetual disagreement.

### Psychological Safety

Psychological safety is the **belief that you won't be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes.** It's the single best predictor of team performance (Google's Project Aristotle).

**Signs of low psychological safety:**

- People don't speak up in meetings
- Mistakes are hidden or blamed
- Questions are treated as weakness
- Disagreements are avoided or become personal

**Signs of high psychological safety:**

- People challenge ideas openly
- Mistakes are shared as learning opportunities
- "I don't know" is an acceptable answer
- Conflict is about ideas, not people

**Building safety:**

- **Model vulnerability** — senior team members admit mistakes first
- **Frame work as learning** — "we don't know if this will work, let's find out"
- **Invite input** — explicitly ask for dissenting opinions
- **Respond well to failure** — investigate what happened, not who caused it

### Trust

Trust on technical teams is built on four dimensions (based on trustworthiness research):

| Dimension | Question | Dev Example |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| **Competence** | Can they do the work? | "They write clean, working code" |
| **Reliability** | Do they follow through? | "They ship what they commit to" |
| **Integrity** | Do they tell the truth? | "They admit when they broke something" |
| **Benevolence** | Do they care about me? | "They help me when I'm stuck" |

**Trust is built incrementally and lost instantly.** A single unreliable delivery erodes months of trust-building.

### Conflict on Teams

Not all conflict is bad. The key distinction:

| Type | Description | Outcome |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Cognitive conflict** | Disagreement about ideas, approaches, methods | Better decisions, innovation |
| **Affective conflict** | Personal friction, dislike, disrespect | Worse decisions, dysfunction |

**Goal: maximize cognitive conflict, minimize affective conflict.**

**Productive disagreement techniques:**

- Argue about specific technical trade-offs, not general approaches
- Use data when possible: "this approach reduces latency by 40%"
- Separate idea from person: "this approach has a problem" not "you're wrong"
- Disagree and commit — after discussion, fully support the decision even if you disagreed
- Time-box disagreements — explore for 30 minutes, then decide
