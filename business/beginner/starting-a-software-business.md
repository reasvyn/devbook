## Content / Material

### Ideation & Validation

Every software business starts with an idea. Most ideas are wrong. The goal of validation is to find the truth before you invest months of engineering time.

**Problem-first, not solution-first.** The best businesses solve real problems. Ask:

- Who experiences this problem?
- How do they solve it today?
- How much does the problem cost them (time, money, frustration)?
- Will they pay to solve it?

**Customer discovery.** Talk to 20–50 potential customers before writing a line of code. Ask open-ended questions about their workflow. Don't pitch — listen. Look for patterns: the same problem mentioned independently by multiple people is a signal.

**The Minimum Viable Product (MVP).** Build the smallest thing that delivers value to early adopters. An MVP is not a prototype — it's a real product with minimal features. Launch to a handful of manual users before automating anything.

**Validation metrics:**

| Signal | What It Means |
|--------|---------------|
| Someone pays | Strongest signal — real willingness to pay |
| Someone uses it regularly | Engagement — they're getting value |
| Someone tells others | Virality potential |
| No one cares after 3 attempts | Pivot or stop |

**The idea maze.** Most startup ideas have been tried before. Study failed attempts in your space — not to be discouraged, but to understand what killed them. Execution matters more than the idea, but a bad idea will kill any execution.

### Legal Structure

Choose a legal structure early. Delaying this creates problems with co-founder equity, fundraising, and taxes.

**Types of entities:**

| Structure | Best For | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| LLC | US-based small businesses, freelancers | Pass-through taxation, flexible, limited liability |
| C-Corp | Startups seeking venture capital | Preferred by VCs, allows unlimited shareholders, double taxation |
| S-Corp | US small businesses with few owners | Pass-through taxation, limited to 100 shareholders |

For most venture-backed software startups, **Delaware C-Corp** is the standard. Investors expect it, and it simplifies future fundraising.

**Incorporation steps:**

1. Choose a company name and check availability
2. File incorporation documents (Delaware is standard for US startups)
3. Draft a founder agreement — roles, responsibilities, vesting
4. Issue founder shares — 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff is industry standard
5. Assign intellectual property to the company
6. Open a business bank account
7. Register for taxes (EIN in the US)

**Co-founder equity.** Split equity early and explicitly. Uneven splits justified by role, experience, and commitment are healthier than "equal by default." Use a vesting schedule so equity is earned over time.

### Fundraising & Finance

**Bootstrapping vs fundraising.** Not every business needs VC money. Consider:

- **Bootstrapping** — you control everything, grow at your pace, keep 100% ownership
- **Angel investment** — $25k–$500k from individuals, often before product-market fit
- **Venture capital** — $500k–$100M+ from funds, for high-growth businesses
- **Grants and accelerators** — non-dilutive funding or small equity (YC, Techstars)

**Fundraising stages:**

| Stage | Typical Amount | Key Milestone |
|-------|---------------|---------------|
| Pre-seed | $100k–$1M | Team, prototype, early traction |
| Seed | $1M–$5M | Product-market fit signal |
| Series A | $5M–$15M | Repeatable growth model |
| Series B | $15M–$50M | Scaling proven business |
| Series C+ | $50M+ | Market dominance, expansion |

**Key metrics investors care about:**

- **ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)** — the standard SaaS metric
- **Growth rate** — month-over-month and year-over-year
- **Net Revenue Retention (NRR)** — >120% means customers pay more over time
- **Gross margin** — >70% for software
- **CAC payback** — <12 months is healthy
- **Burn multiple** — cash burned per dollar of new ARR

**Term sheet basics.** Key terms to understand:

- **Valuation** — pre-money and post-money
- **Liquidation preference** — who gets paid first in an exit
- **Board composition** — how many seats and who controls them
- **Anti-dilution** — protection if later rounds are at a lower valuation
- **Pro-rata rights** — ability to participate in future rounds
- **Information rights** — what you must report to investors

**Financial planning.** Build a simple financial model: revenue projections, hiring plan, operating expenses, and cash flow. Update it monthly. Cash is oxygen — run out of cash and the business dies regardless of how good the product is.

### Team Building

**The founding team.** The ideal founding team has complementary skills: builder (CTO), seller (CEO), and sometimes operator (COO). But many great companies started with just one technical founder who learned to sell.

**First hires.** Hire for attitude and potential, not just experience. Your first 10 employees define the company culture. Look for:

- **Generalists** — they'll wear many hats
- **Self-starters** — no one will tell them what to do
- **Aligned values** — they believe in the mission
- **High ownership** — they care about outcomes, not just tasks

**Company culture.** Culture is what you tolerate. It's not ping-pong tables and free lunch — it's how decisions are made, how conflict is handled, and what happens when someone makes a mistake. Define your values early and reinforce them in every hiring and review process.

**Remote vs office.** Decide early. Remote gives access to global talent and flexibility. Office enables faster collaboration and stronger culture for early-stage teams. Hybrid is the hardest to get right.

**Compensation.** Early-stage compensation typically includes:

- **Below-market salary** — you can't match big tech salaries
- **Meaningful equity** — 0.5–2% for first engineers is common
- **Mission** — people join because they believe in what you're building

### Product Development

**Build the right thing.** The biggest risk in early-stage software is building something nobody wants. Ship quickly, get feedback, iterate. A feature that costs 2 weeks to build is worth 2 days of validation.

**Technical decisions.** Don't over-architect at the start. Choose boring technology — languages and frameworks you know well, with large communities and proven reliability. You can rewrite later once you've found product-market fit. Premature optimization in infrastructure or architecture is the most common technical mistake founders make.

**Quality vs speed.** In the early days, speed wins. Move fast and fix things later — but not never. Accumulating too much technical debt slows you down at exactly the worst time (when you need to scale). Find the balance.

**Product-market fit.** You know you have it when:

- Users are disappointed when your product is down
- Usage grows organically without marketing
- Customers are telling others about you
- You can't build features fast enough

Before product-market fit, do things that don't scale. Talk to every user personally. Build features for individual customers. Do manual work that will later be automated.

### Sales & Marketing

**Founder-led sales.** In the early days, the founder is the salesperson. No one knows the product or the customer better. Selling teaches you what the market actually needs — treat it as part of product discovery, not a separate function.

**Sales motions:**

| Motion | Best For | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Self-serve | Low-price products, PLG | Figma, Notion, Slack |
| Sales-led | High-price enterprise deals | Salesforce, Snowflake |
| Channel | Selling through partners | Embedded payments, ISVs |
| Hybrid | Mid-market | Most B2B SaaS |

**Pricing.** Price is a product feature. Common approaches:

- **Value-based** — what is the product worth to the customer?
- **Competitive** — what are similar products charging?
- **Cost-plus** — what's your cost plus a margin?
- **Tiered** — multiple plans for different segments

Don't underprice. Most founders charge too little. Raising prices later is hard — start higher than feels comfortable.

**Marketing channels.** Focus on one or two channels at the start and go deep before adding more:

- **Content marketing** — blog posts, tutorials, documentation
- **SEO** — ranking for problems your product solves
- **Social** — Twitter/X, LinkedIn, developer communities
- **Paid ads** — Google, LinkedIn, Meta
- **Referrals** — word of mouth from happy customers
- ** Partnerships** — complementary products, resellers

### Operations & Growth

**Unit economics.** Every growing business needs positive unit economics eventually. Know your numbers:

```
CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) = Total Sales & Marketing / New Customers
LTV (Lifetime Value) = Average Revenue Per Customer × Average Customer Lifetime
Gross Margin = (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue
Payback Period = CAC / (Monthly Revenue per Customer × Gross Margin)
```

Healthy SaaS: LTV > 3× CAC, payback < 12 months, gross margin > 70%.

**Metrics that matter.** Don't get lost in vanity metrics. Focus on:

- **Revenue** — MRR, ARR, growth rate
- **Usage** — DAU/MAU, feature adoption, time in product
- **Retention** — churn rate, NRR, cohort retention curves
- **Efficiency** — burn multiple, magic number, CAC ratio

**Customer success.** Retaining customers is cheaper than acquiring new ones. Invest in onboarding, support, and success before scaling sales.

**Scaling challenges.** As you grow, new problems emerge:

- **Process** — what worked with 5 people breaks at 50
- **Communication** — informal coordination doesn't scale
- **Delegation** — you can't make every decision
- **Culture** — new hires dilute existing culture
- **Complexity** — product, codebase, and organization all get more complex

### Exit Strategies

The endgame is not always an IPO. Understand the options so you can build toward the right one.

**Acquisition.** Most successful startup exits are acquisitions. Acquirers buy for:

- **Technology** — your IP and engineering talent (acqui-hire)
- **Team** — your people and culture
- **Product** — your product and customer base
- **Market** — your market position and distribution

**IPO.** Going public is rare and requires scale: typically $100M+ ARR, consistent growth, and public-market readiness. The process involves investment bankers (underwriters), SEC filings (S-1), and a roadshow.

**Secondary sales.** Founders and early employees can sell shares to secondary buyers without a full exit. This provides liquidity while the company stays independent.

**Lifestyle business.** Not every company needs to be a unicorn. A profitable, sustainable business that funds your lifestyle is a perfectly valid outcome.

## Study Cases

**Basecamp (37signals).** Bootstrapped, profitable, and independent for 20+ years. Focused on simple, paid products for small businesses. Proves that VC funding is not required for success.

**Stripe.** Started as a seven-line payment integration. Solved a real problem developers had (accepting payments online was too hard). Grew through developer love and word of mouth before hiring a sales team.

**Figma.** Won the design tools market by building in the browser — no installation, real-time collaboration. Used a generous free tier to drive adoption, then converted teams to paid plans.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Bootstrapping | Building a business without external funding |
| CAC | Customer Acquisition Cost — cost to acquire a paying customer |
| LTV | Lifetime Value — total revenue a customer generates |
| ARR | Annual Recurring Revenue — standard SaaS revenue metric |
| Product-market fit | When a product satisfies a strong market demand |
| Vesting | Earning equity over time (typically 4 years) |
| Term sheet | Non-binding document outlining investment terms |
| Runway | How long a company can operate before running out of cash |
| Churn | Rate at which customers stop paying |
| NRR | Net Revenue Retention — revenue from existing customers including upgrades and churn |
