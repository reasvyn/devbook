# Customer Discovery & Validation

## Description

Customer discovery is the systematic process of identifying who your customers are, what problems they have, and whether they'll pay for a solution. It's the first step in building any product and the skill that separates successful founders from those who build something nobody wants.

## Content / Material

### Why Customer Discovery Matters

The #1 reason startups fail: **building something nobody wants.** Customer discovery is your primary defense against this.

**The cost of being wrong increases over time:**

| Stage | Cost of Wrong Assumption |
|-------|-------------------------|
| Before building | A few hours of interviews |
| After MVP | Weeks of development |
| After launch | Months of engineering, marketing, and sales |
| After funding | Investor capital, team morale, years of your life |

**What discovery teaches you:**

- Who actually has the problem you're solving
- How they currently solve it (your real competition)
- What they'd pay to solve it better
- Whether they'll take action (not just say they're interested)
- Which features matter and which are noise

### The Discovery Mindset

Customer discovery requires a specific mindset that contradicts most founder instincts.

**You are not your customer.** Your intuitions about what people want are probably wrong. The sooner you accept this, the faster you'll find truth.

**Be curious, not defensive.** When a potential customer says your idea won't work, that's data, not criticism. Thank them and dig deeper.

**Fall in love with the problem, not your solution.** Your first solution is almost certainly wrong. The problem is the constant; the solution is the variable.

**Seek disconfirming evidence.** Actively look for reasons your idea won't work. If you can't find any, you haven't looked hard enough.

**The Mom Test principle.** Don't ask if people like your idea. They'll say yes to be polite. Watch what they do, not what they say.

### Identifying Your Hypothesis

Before talking to anyone, write down your assumptions explicitly. These are your **riskiest hypotheses** — the things that must be true for your business to work.

**Customer hypothesis:**

| Assumption | Your Answer |
|------------|-------------|
| Who is the customer? | Be specific — demographics, role, company size, industry |
| What is their problem? | The job they're trying to do but can't do well |
| How do they solve it today? | Current workaround, tool, or process |
| Why is the current solution inadequate? | Pain points, costs, time, quality |
| What would they pay to solve it? | Price range, willingness to pay |

**Value hypothesis:**

| Assumption | Your Answer |
|------------|-------------|
| What is the core value proposition? | In one sentence |
| Why will they buy from you? | Your advantage over alternatives |
| What's the distribution channel? | How will they find out about you? |
| What's the business model? | How will you make money? |
| What are the unit economics? | Rough estimate of LTV and CAC |

**Growth hypothesis:**

| Assumption | Your Answer |
|------------|-------------|
| How will you acquire customers? | Channel strategy |
| What drives repeat usage? | Retention hooks |
| What's the viral coefficient? | How many new users does each user bring? |
| What's the scalability ceiling? | Market size, capacity constraints |

### Finding Interview Subjects

You need to talk to people who represent your target customer. Not your friends, not other founders, not investors.

**Sources for interview subjects:**

- **Your network** — LinkedIn connections, former colleagues, alumni networks
- **Industry events** — conferences, meetups, webinars
- **Online communities** — Reddit, Slack groups, Discord, LinkedIn groups, Quora
- **Cold outreach** — LinkedIn InMail, email, Twitter DMs
- **Customer lists** — if you have existing users or beta testers
- **Referral chains** — ask each interviewee for 2-3 introductions

**How many interviews?**

You need enough to see patterns. Generally:

- **20-30 interviews** for a B2B product with homogeneous customers
- **30-60 interviews** for a B2C product with diverse segments
- **Until patterns repeat** — when you hear the same answers repeatedly, you've reached saturation

**Segment your interviews.** If you're targeting different customer types (e.g., enterprise vs SMB, different industries), interview at least 5-10 from each segment.

### Conducting Customer Interviews

The interview structure that works:

**Preparation (30 minutes):**

- Research the person and their company
- Prepare open-ended questions (no yes/no questions)
- Test your recording setup
- Set a timer for 30 minutes

**Opening (2 minutes):**

- Thank them for their time
- Explain the goal: "I'm trying to understand how you currently handle [problem area]"
- Set expectations: "I won't pitch anything, I'm just here to learn"
- Ask for permission to record

**The interview (25 minutes):**

The goal is to understand their current behavior, not get validation for your idea.

**Good questions:**

- "Walk me through the last time you encountered [problem]."
- "How do you currently handle [situation]?"
- "What's the hardest part about that process?"
- "Have you tried to solve this yourself? What happened?"
- "What tools do you use for this today?"
- "What would happen if you didn't solve this?"
- "What does your ideal solution look like?"
- "How much time/money does this problem cost you?"
- "Who else in your organization deals with this?"
- "What have you tried that didn't work?"

**Bad questions (avoid these):**

- "Would you use a product that does X?" — hypotheticals are worthless
- "How much would you pay for X?" — they don't know until they see it
- "Do you think this is a good idea?" — they'll say yes to be nice
- "If we built X, would you buy it?" — same problem as above
- "Don't you agree that X is a problem?" — leading question

**The most important technique: SHUT UP.** Silence is your best tool. After asking a question, wait. The interviewee will fill the silence with gold. Don't interrupt, don't suggest answers, don't fill the gap.

**Closing (3 minutes):**

- Summarize what you heard
- Ask "Is there anything I should have asked but didn't?"
- Ask for referrals: "Who else should I talk to?"
- Ask for a follow-up if needed

**Post-interview (10 minutes):**

- Transcribe or take detailed notes immediately
- Record key quotes verbatim
- Note patterns, surprises, and contradictions
- Update your hypothesis matrix

### Analyzing Interview Data

After 10-20 interviews, analyze the data systematically.

**Pattern identification:**

| Signal | What It Means |
|--------|---------------|
| Same problem mentioned by 5+ people independently | Real problem worth solving |
| People describe emotional frustration | Problem is painful enough to pay for |
| People have built their own workarounds | Problem is severe |
| People mention competing products | Market exists, needs differentiation |
| Nobody mentions the problem | Either it's not a problem, or you're talking to the wrong people |

**The problem ranking:**

1. List every problem mentioned across all interviews
2. Count how many people mentioned each problem (prevalence)
3. Rate the pain level (1-5) for each problem (intensity)
4. Score = prevalence × intensity
5. Sort by score — this is your priority order

**The customer persona:**

Based on interviews, build a persona of who has the problem most acutely:

- Industry, role, company size
- Their current workflow
- Their budget and authority to purchase
- Their buying process
- Their language and terminology
- What they read, where they congregate online

### Validating Demand

Interviews tell you what people say. Validation tests tell you what people do.

**Validation methods, ranked by strength:**

| Method | Signal Strength | What It Tests |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Pre-order / Deposit** | Strongest | Willingness to pay real money |
| **Letter of Intent** | Strong (B2B) | Enterprise buying intent |
| **Credit card entry form** | Strong | Real purchase intent |
| **Click-through ad to landing page** | Medium | Interest in value proposition |
| **Email signup** | Medium-Low | Casual interest |
| **Interview praise** | Weakest | Politeness, not action |

**The pre-order test:**

Before building anything, create a landing page describing your product and pricing. Include a "Buy Now" button that leads to a "coming soon" page. If people click through and enter their email, you have signal. If they don't, you have a problem.

**The concierge MVP:**

Deliver your solution manually. If you're building software that schedules meetings, do it by hand for 10 customers. If they keep using your manual service, you have demand. If they don't, software won't save you.

**The smoke test:**

Run ads to a landing page that describes your product. Measure click-through rate and signup conversion. A landing page that converts at 10-20% indicates strong interest. Below 5%, your messaging or your product has a problem.

### The Build-Measure-Learn Loop

Customer discovery is not a one-time activity. It's a continuous cycle.

```
Build → Measure → Learn → Build → Measure → Learn → ...
```

**The loop in practice:**

1. **Build** — create the smallest test that produces data (interview guide, landing page, prototype, MVP)
2. **Measure** — collect data (interview notes, conversion rates, usage metrics, churn)
3. **Learn** — compare data against your hypotheses — validated, invalidated, or unclear?
4. **Repeat** — refine your hypothesis, build the next test

**Learning velocity.** The metric that matters is how fast you learn, not how fast you ship. A week of interviews that invalidates a hypothesis is more valuable than a month of building the wrong product.

**Decision points after each loop:**

| Learning Outcome | Action |
|------------------|--------|
| Hypothesis validated | Invest more, build the real product |
| Hypothesis partially validated | Refine hypothesis, test again |
| Hypothesis invalidated | Pivot or kill the idea |
| No clear signal | Better experiments needed |

### Pivoting vs Persevering

A pivot is a structured change in strategy based on what you've learned. It's not failure — it's learning applied.

**Types of pivots:**

| Pivot Type | What Changes | Example |
|------------|--------------|---------|
| **Customer segment** | You solve the same problem for different users | B2C → B2B |
| **Problem pivot** | Same customers, different problem | Photo sharing → photo editing |
| **Solution pivot** | Same problem, different solution | Mobile app → web app |
| **Technology pivot** | Same solution, different tech | Cloud → on-premise |
| **Channel pivot** | Same customers, different sales channel | Direct sales → partnerships |
| **Business model pivot** | Same product, different monetization | Subscription → usage-based |

**How to know it's time to pivot:**

- You've talked to 30+ potential customers and nobody has the problem
- You've tested 3+ different solutions and none gained traction
- Your retention curve drops to zero after 30 days
- Users say they want it but nobody pays
- You're running out of money and the current path won't work

**How to know it's time to persevere:**

- You have a small group of passionate users
- Your metrics are improving, even if slowly
- Customers are telling others about you
- You have clear next experiments to run
- You can articulate what you'd change and why

### Common Mistakes

**Confirmation bias.** You hear what you want to hear. You interpret ambiguous answers as validation. You stop interviewing after 3 people said yes.

**Solutioneering.** You fell in love with your solution and you're looking for a problem to attach it to. Start with the problem, not the idea.

**Talking to the wrong people.** You interviewed friends, family, and other founders who don't represent your target customer. They were polite. Their answers mean nothing.

**Pitching instead of listening.** You spent the interview convincing them your idea is great instead of understanding their problem. The data is worthless.

**Not following up on pain.** Someone mentions a problem. You don't ask "how much does this cost you?" or "what have you tried?" You miss the chance to gauge severity.

**Stopping too early.** You did 5 interviews and saw a pattern. You declare victory and start building. But was it a real pattern or just noise? Keep going until you see saturation.

**Over-relying on quantitative data.** Metrics tell you what, not why. You see high churn but don't understand it. Metrics without interviews are blind.

**Not updating your hypotheses.** You wrote down assumptions once and never revisited them. Every interview should refine your understanding.

**Fear of bad news.** You avoid asking hard questions because you don't want to hear "no." The hard questions are the only ones that matter.

**Analysis paralysis.** You keep interviewing and never build anything. At some point, you have enough data to act. Trust your judgment and move forward.

## Study Cases

### Case 1: The Wrong Problem

A founder of a restaurant management platform interviewed 20 restaurant owners. Every owner said they had trouble with inventory management. The founder built a beautiful inventory system. Nobody used it.

What went wrong: The founder asked "Do you have trouble with inventory?" and everyone said yes. But when asked "How much time does inventory cost you per week?" the answer was "about 30 minutes." It wasn't painful enough to pay for.

What they should have discovered: The real pain was customer acquisition. Restaurants struggled to fill empty tables. A waitlist and reservation system was worth paying for.

### Case 2: The Wrong Customer

A B2B SaaS founder built a project management tool for small agencies. After 12 months of struggle, they interviewed their customers and discovered most users were in IT departments of large enterprises — a completely different segment.

What changed: They repositioned the product for IT operations, added enterprise features (SSO, audit logs, compliance), and grew 5× in the next year. The original product was fine. The market was wrong.

## Examples

### Example: The Mom Test in Practice

**Bad interview:**

```
Founder: "Would you use an app that helps you track your team's 
         OKRs and align them with company goals?"
Customer: "That sounds useful, I'd definitely use that."
Founder: "Great! How much would you pay for it?"
Customer: "Maybe $10/month per user."
```

This interview tells you nothing. The customer was polite.

**Good interview:**

```
Founder: "Walk me through how your team currently sets and tracks goals."
Customer: "We use a spreadsheet but nobody updates it after the first month."
Founder: "Why do you think that happens?"
Customer: "It takes too long and nobody checks it anyway."
Founder: "What happens when goals aren't tracked?"
Customer: "We realize halfway through the quarter that we're 
         working on the wrong things."
Founder: "How much time did that cost you last quarter?"
Customer: "Probably 2 weeks of work from a 5-person team."
Founder: "That's $10-15K. What have you tried to fix this?"
Customer: "We looked at Asana but it was too complex for just goals."
```

This interview reveals: real problem (wasted time), current solution (spreadsheet), why it fails (too much work), what they tried (Asana but rejected), and the cost ($10-15K/quarter). This is actionable data.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Customer discovery | The process of identifying customer needs through direct research |
| Riskiest hypothesis | The assumption that must be true for your business to work |
| Saturation | The point where additional interviews stop producing new insights |
| Concierge MVP | Manually delivering your solution to validate demand before building software |
| Smoke test | Testing demand by measuring response to a landing page or ad |
| Pivot | A structured change in strategy based on validated learning |
| The Mom Test | Avoiding polite lies by asking about past behavior, not future intentions |
| Solutioneering | Falling in love with a solution before validating the problem |
| Build-Measure-Learn | The iterative cycle of building experiments, measuring results, and learning |
| Learning velocity | The speed at which you validate or invalidate assumptions |

**Before PMF, every interview is gold.** Your job is to conduct enough good interviews to see patterns clearly, then act on what you learn — even when it means abandoning your original idea. The startups that succeed aren't the ones with the best initial idea. They're the ones that learn fastest and iterate effectively.

**After PMF, discovery continues.** Successful companies never stop talking to customers. The questions change — from "what problem do you have?" to "what's still painful? what's missing? what would make you love us?" — but the practice remains. Customer discovery is not a phase. It's a permanent capability.

**The discovery maturity model:**

| Stage | Approach | Cadence | Output |
|-------|----------|---------|--------|
| **Zero** | Founder intuition | None | Product built on assumptions |
| **Ad hoc** | Occasional customer calls | Monthly | Random feature ideas |
| **Systematic** | Regular customer interviews | Weekly | Prioritized problem list |
| **Continuous** | Integrated into product process | Daily | Real-time customer insights driving roadmap |

**Every startup should aim for Stage 3 (systematic) before raising Series A, and Stage 4 (continuous) after.** At Stage 4, customer discovery is not a separate activity — it's how the company operates. Every product decision is informed by direct customer input. Every experiment starts with a hypothesis derived from customer conversations.

**The tools of systematic discovery:**
- Customer interview calendar (2 interviews per week minimum)
- Shared interview notes database (Notion, Airtable)
- Problem prioritization board (what to solve next)
- Quarterly customer advisory board (10 key customers, 90 minutes)
- Annual customer conference or survey (broad input)
