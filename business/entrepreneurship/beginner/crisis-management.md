# Crisis Management

## Description

Crisis management is how a startup responds to existential threats — running out of cash, security breaches, PR disasters, team breakdowns, and regulatory actions. For founders, a crisis is inevitable. The only question is whether you've prepared for it.

## Content / Material

### Why Crisis Management Matters

Crises are inevitable in startups. A company that survives 10 years will face multiple existential threats. The difference between companies that survive and those that die is not luck — it's preparation.

**Why startups are vulnerable to crises:**

- No cash reserves (most run with < 6 months runway)
- No crisis experience (first-time founders have never navigated a downturn)
- No redundancy systems (single points of failure everywhere)
- No PR team (the founder handles communications)
- No legal resources (can't afford on-call counsel)
- High stress environment (bad decisions under pressure)

**The cost of a mishandled crisis:**

| Unmanaged Crisis | Managed Crisis |
|-----------------|----------------|
| Company fails | Company survives |
| Reputation destroyed | Reputation strengthened |
| Customers churn | Customers trust you more |
| Team falls apart | Team rallies together |
| Founder burns out | Founder grows stronger |

### The Crisis Mindset

**Assume the crisis will happen.** Not "if" but "when." This isn't pessimism — it's preparation. If you've already considered the scenario, you'll react faster when it happens.

**Bad news doesn't get better with time.** The hardest rule of crisis management: communicate bad news as early as possible. Every hour you delay is an hour that rumors fill the information vacuum. Your stakeholders will imagine the worst case. Get ahead of it.

**Focus on what you can control.** In a crisis, most of what's happening is outside your control. Your response is inside your control. Focus there.

**Separation of concerns.** One person handles the crisis response. Another person keeps the business running. The founder usually handles the crisis, but if you're the founder who needs to keep the company running during a cash crisis, delegate the crisis to your co-founder.

**The 80/20 rule in a crisis.** You don't need a perfect response. You need a good enough response, fast. A good decision today beats a perfect decision next week.

### Cash Crisis

A cash crisis happens when the company will run out of money before it can raise more or become profitable. It's the most common existential crisis in startups.

**Signs of a cash crisis:**

- Runway < 6 months and fundraising is not progressing
- Revenue growth is slower than burn
- Major customer that represents > 20% of revenue is churning
- Investors are signaling that the next round won't come
- You're delaying vendor payments

**The first step: extend runway immediately.**

| Action | Impact | Speed |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Founder salary to minimum | Extends runway 1-3 months | Immediate |
| Cut non-essential vendors | Extends runway 1-2 months | 1-2 weeks |
| Freeze hiring | Slows burn rate | Immediate |
| Renegotiate contracts | Extends runway 1-2 months | 1-2 months |
| Reduce office/cloud spend | Extends runway 1-2 weeks | Immediate |
| Pause all non-critical projects | Focuses resources | Immediate |

**The second step: assess options.**

| Option | When It Works | Risks |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| **Revenue acceleration** | You have product-market fit and can sell more | Takes time, uncertain outcome |
| **Bridge round** | Existing investors believe in the company | Dilution, signals distress |
| **Down round** | Company is viable but needs more time | Massive dilution, morale hit |
| **Convertible note bridge** | Fastest way to get money | Debt if it doesn't convert |
| **Strategic investment** | Large company sees value in your tech | Loss of control, strategic constraints |
| **Asset sale** | You have valuable IP or data | Selling the crown jewels |
| **Acquisition** | Another company wants your team or tech | Loss of independence |
| **Shut down** | No viable path forward | Company ends |

**How to evaluate options:**

Create a decision matrix:

```
Option: Bridge round
  Probability of success: 70%
  Time to execute: 2-3 months
  Dilution: 10-20%
  Post-money scenario: 12 more months of runway
  Downside: Signals distress to future investors

Option: Revenue acceleration
  Probability of success: 40%
  Time to execute: 3-6 months
  Dilution: 0%
  Post-money scenario: Company becomes profitable
  Downside: May not work in time
```

**Layoffs as a last resort:**

If you must do layoffs, do them right.

1. **Do it once, not in waves.** Multiple layoffs destroy morale. If you need to cut 20%, cut 25% to avoid a second round.
2. **Do it fast.** From decision to execution, 24-48 hours. Dragging it out creates anxiety and leaks.
3. **Communicate clearly.** Why it happened, who is affected, what the severance package is, what the plan is going forward.
4. **Treat people with dignity.** Good severance, outplacement support, honest references.
5. **Lead the communication yourself.** As the founder, you own the message. Don't delegate it to HR.

**The founder's salary during cash crisis:**

The first cut should always be the founder's salary. Zero yourself before cutting anyone else. This signals that you're in it together and you're taking the hit first.

**Surviving the cash crisis timeline:**

```
Week 1: Assess — measure runway, build scenarios
Week 2: Cut — reduce burn to essentials
Week 3: Raise — bridge round or revenue push
Week 4: Communicate — team, board, key stakeholders
Month 2-3: Execute — ship product, close deals
Month 4-6: Stabilize — positive unit economics or funding secured
```

### Security Breach

A security breach is one of the most stressful events a founder will face. Customer data exposed, trust broken, legal liability incurred.

**Immediate response (first 24 hours):**

| Priority | Action |
|----------|--------|
| **Stop the bleeding** | Take the affected system offline, revoke compromised credentials, block the attack vector |
| **Preserve evidence** | Capture logs, snapshots, and system state for forensics |
| **Notify key stakeholders** | Legal counsel, board, co-founders |
| **Assess the scope** | What data was accessed? How many users affected? |
| **Engage experts** | Forensic security firm, breach counsel |

**Notification requirements:**

| Jurisdiction | Regulation | Notification Timeline |
|--------------|------------|----------------------|
| US (general) | Varies by state | 30-60 days |
| California | CCPA | Without unreasonable delay |
| EU | GDPR | 72 hours |
| UK | DPA 2018 | 72 hours |
| All | Contractual MSA/DPA obligations | Per agreement terms |

**Customer communication:**

| Audience | Message | Channel |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **All users** | What happened, data affected, what you're doing | Email + in-app |
| **Affected users** | Detailed impact, specific steps to take | Direct email |
| **Enterprise customers** | Technical details, compliance impact | Direct call from founder |
| **Press / public** | Acknowledgment, investigation status, commitment | Blog post |

**Customer communication template:**

```
Subject: Security incident — what happened and what we're doing

We discovered [date] that [description of incident]. [X] user accounts
were affected. The following data was accessed: [types of data].

What we've done:
- [Action taken to stop the breach]
- [Action to prevent recurrence]
- [Forensic investigation underway]

What you should do:
- [Specific actions — change password, enable 2FA, etc.]

We're deeply sorry. We take data security seriously and will share
the full investigation results when complete.
```

**The post-mortem (after containment):**

1. Root cause analysis — how did this happen?
2. Timeline — when did the attack start, when was it detected, how long was the exposure?
3. Remediation — what changed to prevent recurrence?
4. Lessons learned — what would you do differently?
5. Public post-mortem — publish for transparency and trust

### PR Crisis

A PR crisis is a sudden threat to your company's reputation. It can be triggered by a product failure, a founder's public statement, a customer complaint going viral, or an employee's actions.

**Types of PR crisis:**

| Type | Example | Severity |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Product failure** | Extended outage, data loss | High |
| **Founder controversy** | Public statement, past behavior resurfacing | Very high |
| **Customer complaint** | Bad experience goes viral | Medium |
| **Employee issue** | Discrimination, harassment | Very high |
| **Partner crisis** | Supplier or partner does something bad | Medium |
| **Industry backlash** | Product has negative externalities | High |

**The PR crisis response framework:**

| Phase | Timeframe | Actions |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Acknowledge** | 1-4 hours | Confirm awareness, express concern, promise update |
| **Investigate** | 4-24 hours | Gather facts, understand scope, assess impact |
| **Respond** | 24-48 hours | Public statement, specific actions, commitments |
| **Implement** | 48 hours+ | Execute remediation, monitor sentiment |
| **Follow up** | 1-4 weeks | Post-mortem, policy changes, rebuild trust |

**PR crisis principles:**

- **Acknowledge fast.** Even if you don't have all the facts. "We're aware and investigating."
- **Be transparent.** Share what you know, what you don't know, and when you'll know more.
- **Show empathy.** Acknowledge the impact on customers, employees, and the community.
- **Take action.** Commit to specific steps. Follow through.
- **Don't speculate.** If you don't know whether user data was affected, say "we're still investigating."
- **No "no comment."** It reads as guilt. If you can't comment, explain why.

**Social media during a crisis:**

- Monitor constantly — social media is where the story develops
- Respond quickly — don't leave questions unanswered for hours
- Stay on brand — your crisis voice should be human, humble, and direct
- Don't argue — engaging with critics is rarely productive
- Know when to go private — some conversations should move to DMs

### Team Crisis

Team crises can be more devastating than financial ones. A founding team breakup, a key person leaving, or a toxic culture can destroy the company from within.

**Co-founder departure:**

| Situation | Impact | Response |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Co-founder quits** | Loss of skill, share, morale | Vesting cliff protects company. Buy back shares. Communicate to team |
| **Co-founder must be removed** | Complex, emotional, legal | Lawyer up. Follow vesting agreements. Protect company interests |
| **Co-founder becomes toxic** | Team morale, productivity | Immediate intervention. If irreconcilable, separate |

**Co-founder separation agreement elements:**

1. **Equity treatment** — vested shares, unvested shares, buyback terms
2. **Role transition** — what happens to their responsibilities
3. **Non-compete** — can they start a competing company?
4. **IP ownership** — confirm the company owns everything they built
5. **Communication** — how to announce to team, investors, customers
6. **Transition period** — how long they stay to help transition

**Key person risk:**

| Role | Risk If They Leave | Mitigation |
|------|-------------------|------------|
| **CTO / Lead engineer** | Product development stops or slows | Cross-training, documentation |
| **Head of Sales** | Revenue pipeline collapses | CRM data, account ownership documented |
| **Founder** | Company identity crisis | Succession plan, board involvement |

**The "bus factor":** the number of people who, if hit by a bus, would cripple the company. Anything below 2 is dangerous. Cross-train critical roles.

**Toxic culture response:**

| Symptom | Intervention | Timeline |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Bullying or harassment** | Investigation, potential termination | Hours to days |
| **Exclusion or bias** | Training, process changes, accountability | Weeks to months |
| **Burnout** | Reduce workload, add resources, set boundaries | Weeks |
| **Silos and politics** | Reorganization, communication changes, coaching | Months |

**Team communication during crisis:**

- **Frequent updates.** Daily if needed. Silence creates anxiety.
- **Be honest.** Don't sugarcoat. The team knows when things are bad.
- **Ask for help.** "We're in a tough spot and I need everyone focused."
- **Protect the team.** If external crisis, shield them. If internal, bring them in.

### Regulatory & Legal Crisis

Regulatory and legal crises can come from government action, lawsuits, or newly applicable regulations.

**Types of regulatory crisis:**

| Type | Example | Warning Signs |
|------|---------|---------------|
| **Regulatory investigation** | FTC, SEC inquiry | Subpoena, information request |
| **Class action lawsuit** | Customers or employees sue | Plaintiff firm ads, social media |
| **IP dispute** | Patent or copyright claim | Cease and desist letter |
| **Employment dispute** | Wrongful termination, discrimination | Legal demand, complaint |
| **Tax issue** | Back taxes, incorrect classification | Audit notice |

**Immediate steps:**

1. **Notify legal counsel.** Do not respond without a lawyer.
2. **Preserve all relevant data.** Legal hold on documents, emails, messages.
3. **Assess the exposure.** What's the worst case — fine, settlement, shutdown?
4. **Communicate internally on a need-to-know basis.**
5. **Do not discuss publicly.** "We cannot comment on pending litigation."

**Building a relationship with legal counsel:**

| Stage | Legal Needs | Cost |
|-------|-------------|------|
| Pre-seed | Incorporation, IP, founder agreements | $5-15K |
| Seed | Fundraising docs, customer contracts, employment | $10-30K/month |
| Series A+ | Complex contracts, compliance, litigation readiness | $20-50K+/month |

**Relating to regulators:**

- Be cooperative, not adversarial — obstruction makes it worse
- Respond on time — every deadline is real
- Be transparent — share more than required, it builds trust
- Don't lie — lying to a regulator is a crime
- Hire experts — for complex issues, get specialized counsel

### Crisis Communication

Crisis communication is the most visible part of crisis management. How you communicate during a crisis determines whether stakeholders trust your response.

**The crisis communication plan:**

Every startup should have a crisis communication plan before a crisis happens.

| Element | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Crisis team** | Who makes decisions? Who communicates? |
| **Spokesperson** | Who speaks publicly? (usually the CEO) |
| **Approval process** | Who approves external communications? |
| **Channel strategy** | Where do you communicate? (blog, email, social, press) |
| **Template statements** | Draft statements for common crisis types |
| **Stakeholder list** | Who needs to be contacted and in what order |
| **Monitoring plan** | How do you track the story? |

**Stakeholder communication order:**

1. **Internal team** — before anyone else (1-2 hours)
2. **Board of directors** — next (2-4 hours)
3. **Affected customers** — directly impacted (4-12 hours)
4. **All customers** — broad communication (12-24 hours)
5. **Investors** — after you have a clear picture (24-48 hours)
6. **Partners and vendors** — as needed (24-48 hours)
7. **Press and public** — after you have a response ready (24-72 hours)

**The crisis statement structure:**

```
[Title — clear and direct]

We're writing to share an important update about [incident].

What happened:
[Clear, factual description]

Who is affected:
[Specifics about data or services impacted]

What we've done:
[Immediate actions taken]

What we're doing next:
[Long-term remediation]

What you should do:
[Actions stakeholders should take]

We're sorry:
[Genuine acknowledgment of impact]
```

**Internal communication during crisis:**

Your team is your most important audience. Keep them informed.

- **Daily updates** — even "nothing new to report" maintains trust
- **Open Q&A** — give them space to ask questions
- **Address rumors directly** — "I've heard X. Here's the truth."
- **Show leadership** — "I know this is hard. Here's our plan."
- **Protect their focus** — "Your job is to keep building. I'm handling this."

### Post-Mortem & Recovery

After the crisis is contained, the work of understanding and improving begins.

**The post-mortem:**

A post-mortem is a blameless analysis of what happened and what to do differently.

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Summary** | One paragraph overview |
| **Timeline** | Detailed chronology of events |
| **Root cause** | The underlying cause (not just the trigger) |
| **Impact** | Quantitative impact (revenue lost, customers affected) |
| **Response assessment** | What went well, what went wrong |
| **Action items** | Specific changes to prevent recurrence |
| **Owners and deadlines** | Who is responsible for each action item |

**Blameless post-mortem principles:**

- Assume everyone did their best with the information they had
- Focus on systems, not individuals
- Ask "How did our system allow this?" not "Who made the mistake?"
- The goal is learning, not accountability

**Recovery phases:**

| Phase | Duration | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **Stabilize** | Days | Stop the bleeding, secure the business |
| **Rebuild** | Weeks | Fix systems, restore processes, regain team confidence |
| **Restore trust** | Months | Customer relationships, investor confidence |
| **Strengthen** | Quarters | Build resilience, institutionalize lessons |

**Rebuilding trust with customers:**

- Public post-mortem — transparency builds trust
- Direct apology from founder — personal responsibility shows leadership
- Compensation (credits, discounts) — tangible acknowledgment of impact
- Feature improvements — show you fixed the root cause
- Regular updates — keep customers informed of progress

**Founder recovery:**

A crisis takes a toll on the founder. Recovery is essential.

- Debrief with co-founder or board member — process what happened
- Take time off (even 2-3 days) — mental reset
- Talk to other founders who've been through it — peer support
- Document the lessons — you won't remember everything later
- Be kind to yourself — you did your best in an impossible situation

### Common Mistakes

**Waiting too long to communicate.** The #1 crisis management mistake. Bad news doesn't age well. Communicate as soon as you know something is wrong.

**Hiding bad news from the team.** The team always finds out. They'll trust you less if they hear it from outside sources.

**Responding to every critic.** Not every critic deserves a response. Focus on constructive feedback, ignore the noise.

**Making promises you can't keep.** "We'll fix everything in 24 hours." If you can't deliver, trust damage is worse. Under-promise and over-deliver.

**Going it alone.** Founders isolate themselves during crisis. Bring in help — board members, advisors, mentors, therapists. You don't have to face it alone.

**No crisis plan.** A pre-written crisis plan, even if imperfect, is better than improvising under pressure.

**Failing to learn.** After the crisis passes, the temptation is to move on and never think about it again. The lessons are your most valuable asset. Institutionalize them.

**Legal-first, people-second.** Your lawyer tells you to say nothing. Your instinct says to communicate. Find the balance — protect your legal position while treating people with respect.

## Study Cases

### Case 1: The Cash Crisis Caught Early

A 60-person startup had 8 months of runway. The founder noticed sales slowing for 3 consecutive weeks. Instead of hoping it was variance, he acted: froze hiring, cut marketing spend by 50%, and renegotiated cloud contracts. Total burn reduction: 30%. Runway extended from 8 to 14 months. Sales recovered in month 4 after a product improvement. The company never needed to do layoffs.

Lesson: Act early when data signals a problem. The sooner you address a cash crisis, the more options you have.

### Case 2: The Breach That Built Trust

A SaaS startup suffered a data breach affecting 5,000 customers. The CEO sent a personal email to every affected customer within 12 hours, explaining exactly what happened, what data was accessed, and what they were doing. She followed up with daily updates for 2 weeks, then a full public post-mortem with technical details and remediation steps. Customer retention was 97%. NPS scores actually increased after the incident.

Lesson: Honest, transparent communication during a crisis can strengthen customer relationships — even when the crisis is your fault.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Runway | Months until the company runs out of cash |
| Bridge round | Short-term funding to extend runway until the next priced round |
| Down round | A funding round at a lower valuation than the previous round |
| Layoff | Termination of employees due to business necessity |
| Post-mortem | A blameless analysis of an incident to identify root causes and prevent recurrence |
| Bus factor | The number of people whose departure would cripple the company |
| Legal hold | A directive to preserve all relevant documents and data for potential litigation |
| Dunning | Automated process to recover failed payments (relevant during cash crisis) |
| Crisis communication plan | A pre-written playbook for communicating with stakeholders during emergencies |
