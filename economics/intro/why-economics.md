# Why Economics?

## Description

Economics is the science of decision-making under scarcity. For developers, it explains why users behave the way they do, how markets clear, and how to reason about trade-offs in product and system design.

## Prerequisites

- No formal prerequisites — just curiosity about how people and systems make choices

## Content / Material

### What Is Economics?

Economics studies how people, firms, and governments allocate limited resources to satisfy unlimited wants. It splits into two branches:

- **Microeconomics** — individual agents: consumers, firms, markets
- **Macroeconomics** — aggregates: inflation, unemployment, GDP growth

The core problem is always the same: **we can't have everything, so we must choose**.

### Why Developers Should Care

- **Incentives drive behavior** — understanding why users, teammates, and executives act the way they do
- **Trade-offs are everywhere** — every technical decision (speed vs quality, build vs buy, monolith vs microservices) is an economic problem
- **Market design** — platforms, marketplaces, and two-sided networks are economic mechanisms implemented in code
- **Pricing** — subscription tiers, freemium, usage-based billing are all economic strategies encoded in software
- **Resource allocation** — engineering time, cloud budget, and infrastructure capacity are scarce resources

### The Economic Mindset

| Principle | Meaning | Dev Analogy |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Scarcity | Resources are limited | You have 5 engineers and 20 features to build |
| Opportunity cost | The next best thing you give up | Choosing React means not choosing Vue |
| Marginal analysis | Decide at the margin — one more unit | Should we add one more feature or ship now? |
| Incentives | People respond to incentives | Higher latency drives users away; faster response retains them |
| Trade | Voluntary exchange benefits both sides | Open-source contributions benefit maintainer and contributor |

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Scarcity | The fundamental economic problem of having limited resources to meet unlimited wants |
| Opportunity cost | The value of the next best alternative foregone when making a decision |
| Marginal analysis | Examining the additional benefit of one more unit of an activity |
| Microeconomics | Study of individual economic agents and markets |
| Macroeconomics | Study of economy-wide phenomena like inflation and growth |
